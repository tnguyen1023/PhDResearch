\begin{abstract}
Deciding where to place honeypots in a large enterprise network is a
hard combinatorial optimisation problem. Enterprise networks are divided
into security zones with different trust levels, contain diverse server
types, and face adversaries who follow structured kill-chain attack
patterns. Budget constraints, pairwise honeypot conflicts, and physical
air gaps jointly restrict the set of feasible deployments, making ad-hoc
or greedy placement strategies insufficient for comprehensive detection.

We model honeypot placement as a Weighted Partial Maximum
Satisfiability (\MAXSAT{}) problem with six simultaneous objectives:
\emph{detection efficiency}~(O1), \emph{ATT\&CK technique coverage}~(O2),
\emph{tactic-family breadth}~(O3), \emph{early attacker interception}~(O4),
\emph{forensic backward coverage}~(O5), and \emph{multi-path
coverage}~(O6). Each objective addresses a distinct failure mode of prior
methods. All hard constraints are encoded as inviolable clauses.
We establish NP-hardness via polynomial-time reduction from Weighted
Maximum Coverage and solve with the RC2 core-guided solver, which
provides an optimality certificate for instances that converge within
the time budget.

We evaluate across six synthetic network scales (100 to 5.56~million
nodes) and conduct proof-of-concept emulation on a five-zone
Mininet/OpenFlow testbed running real Cowrie SSH and Conpot ICS
honeypots. At the 500\,000-node scale our solver achieves $Q=71.39$
versus $Q=53.69$ for the strongest baseline ($+17.70$~pts, $+32.9\%$).
Emulation confirms that solver placements are deployable and
directionally superior to greedy alternatives, subject to the
limitations discussed in Section~\ref{sec:limitations}.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:intro}
%% =======================================================================

\subsection{Why Honeypot Placement Matters}
A honeypot is a deliberately exposed computing resource whose sole
purpose is to attract adversaries. Because no legitimate user should
interact with it, any connection is a strong indicator of malicious
activity. When placed well, a honeypot can detect an attacker early
in the kill chain and buy defenders time to respond. When placed
poorly, it wastes budget and produces no actionable intelligence.

Placement quality matters more than deployment volume. A network
operator with a fixed budget must decide not only \emph{how many}
honeypots to deploy but also \emph{which types} to use and \emph{where}
to place them across a heterogeneous, multi-zone enterprise topology.
These decisions interact: a single SSH honeypot in the DMZ can be far
more valuable than ten generic traps in a low-traffic internal subnet.
No operational heuristic reliably identifies the optimal combination
when hundreds of server types, thousands of network nodes, and multiple
concurrent attack paths must be considered simultaneously.

\subsection{Enterprise Constraints}
Real enterprise networks are partitioned into security zones---DMZ,
internal LAN, cloud, OT/SCADA, and management---each with distinct
trust levels and permitted traffic flows. Air-gap constraints isolate
the most sensitive zones, making certain honeypot types physically
unreachable from specific locations. Budget constraints limit total
deployment cost. Pairwise conflicts arise when two honeypot types
would produce redundant signals or saturate a zone's monitoring
pipeline. Together, these constraints---budget limits, zone isolation,
pairwise conflicts, and air gaps---make the placement problem NP-hard
(Theorem~\ref{thm:nph}).

\subsection{Kill Chains and Structured Objectives}
Sophisticated attackers follow structured kill chains~\cite{hutchins2011}
and employ techniques catalogued in MITRE ATT\&CK~\cite{mitre2023},
moving step by step from initial access toward their target. This has
two implications for placement. First, placement must account for the
attacker's likely \emph{path}: a honeypot at the perimeter (hop~1)
prevents compromise; one at the penultimate hop only records it after
the fact. Second, attackers may change techniques or paths to avoid
detection, so a resilient placement must cover diverse ATT\&CK
techniques and multiple concurrent kill-chain paths simultaneously.

Ad-hoc strategies cannot address these requirements jointly. They
address at most one or two objectives at a time and cannot reason
about global interactions among constraints, path probabilities, and
technique coverage.

\subsection{Our Approach: Six Objectives, One Solver}
We argue that a satisfactory placement framework must address six
distinct security objectives simultaneously:

\begin{enumerate}[label=O\arabic*.]
  \item \textbf{Detection Efficiency}: cover high-value assets in
    attack-active zones.
  \item \textbf{ATT\&CK Technique Coverage}: cover a diverse range of
    MITRE ATT\&CK techniques so that no technique offers a blind spot.
  \item \textbf{Tactic-Family Breadth}: cover multiple ATT\&CK tactic
    families so that an attacker who shifts stages remains detectable.
  \item \textbf{Early Interception}: prioritise placements that catch
    the attacker before the final hop, favouring prevention over
    forensics.
  \item \textbf{Forensic Backward Coverage}: provide a secondary
    forensic layer at later hops, discounted to avoid over-investment
    in post-compromise detection.
  \item \textbf{Multi-Path Coverage}: simultaneously encode all
    candidate attack paths to prevent over-investment in the
    highest-probability path.
\end{enumerate}

We encode all six objectives and all hard constraints as a single
Weighted Partial \MAXSAT{} instance solved by the RC2
solver~\cite{rc2}.

\subsection{Contributions}
\begin{enumerate}
  \item \textbf{Six-objective \MAXSAT{} encoding} with clause-weight
    justifications and a necessity argument for each objective
    (Section~\ref{sec:model}).
  \item \textbf{NP-hardness proof} via reduction from Weighted Maximum
    Coverage~\cite{feige1998} (Section~\ref{sec:np}).
  \item \textbf{Cross-path force-multiplier analysis} showing
    ${\approx}2.1\times$ per-decision weight advantage over greedy
    strategies (Section~\ref{sec:forcemult}).
  \item \textbf{Zone-decomposed scalable solver} reaching 5.56M nodes
    in ${\approx}30$~s (Section~\ref{sec:solver}).
  \item \textbf{Proof-of-concept emulation} with Cowrie and Conpot on
    a Mininet/OpenFlow testbed (Section~\ref{sec:emulation}).
\end{enumerate}

\section{Related Work}
\label{sec:related}
%% =======================================================================

We organise prior work around five capability dimensions:
\textbf{D1}~multi-zone topology, \textbf{D2}~kill-chain ordering,
\textbf{D3}~ATT\&CK integration, \textbf{D4}~budget and conflict hard
constraints, and \textbf{D5}~proven optimality.
Table~\ref{tab:related_obj} maps each method against our six objectives.

\paragraph{Foundational honeypot work}
Provos~\cite{provos2004} and Spitzner~\cite{spitzner2003} established
the conceptual foundations of honeynets but assumed manual expert
placement with no automation, budget model, or kill-chain awareness.
Yuill et al.~\cite{yuill2004} extended the concept to deceptive files
(honeyfiles) for intrusion detection. Wagener et
al.~\cite{wagener2009} proposed adaptive self-configuring honeypots,
and Fraunholz et al.~\cite{fraunholz2017} characterised deployment
challenges specific to industrial control systems. None address
automated multi-zone placement under budget and conflict constraints.

\paragraph{Game-theoretic frameworks}
Kiekintveld et al.~\cite{kiekintveld2015} proved that mixed strategies
dominate deterministic placement against rational adversaries; Pawlick
et al.~\cite{pawlick2019} provided a comprehensive deception taxonomy.
Wang et al.~\cite{wang2018} surveyed cyber deception and identified
honeypot placement as a key open problem. Achleitner et
al.~\cite{achleitner2017} used virtual networks to defend against
insider reconnaissance. Tan et al.~\cite{tan2011} explored
network-layer obliviousness as a complementary deception primitive.
All assume flat networks without zone isolation (D1), kill-chain
ordering (D2), ATT\&CK integration (D3), or conflict constraints (D4).

\paragraph{Reinforcement learning}
Huang and Zhu~\cite{huang2019} optimise honeypot \emph{engagement}
policies after placement is fixed. Their work is complementary: our
solver decides \emph{where} to place; their RL decides \emph{how} to
engage attackers that arrive.

\paragraph{ATT\&CK-based methods}
Applebaum et al.~\cite{applebaum2016} used ATT\&CK for red-team
emulation but produced no placement algorithm. Hemberg
et al.~\cite{hemberg2018} applied a genetic algorithm to ATT\&CK
control selection but evaluated one objective at a time, preventing
discovery of cross-path synergies.

\paragraph{Formal methods}
Saha and Gera~\cite{saha2015} achieved proven-optimal ILP sensor
placement but without kill-chain (D2) or ATT\&CK (D3) objectives and
limited to ${\approx}50\,000$ nodes. Pinisetty et
al.~\cite{pinisetty2017} applied \MAXSAT{} to CPS runtime enforcement
without zone or kill-chain modelling; Liffiton and
Malik~\cite{liffiton2016} developed efficient MUS enumeration
underlying RC2's core-guided search. Jajodia et al.~\cite{jajodia2005}
motivate our path-weight formula via chokepoint analysis but provide no
placement algorithm.

\paragraph{Policy and access-control methods}
Al-Shaer and Hamed~\cite{alshaer2004}, Bak et al.~\cite{bak2009}, and
Fisler et al.~\cite{fisler2005} developed formal policy verification
and firewall management tools that share our motivation for formal
specification of network security constraints. Bozorgi et
al.~\cite{bozorgi2010} applied learning methods to vulnerability
classification, informing the technique stealth scores $\sigma_j$ used
in our weight formulation~\eqref{eq:stealth}.

\subsection{Formal Problem Statement}

A honeypot placement instance is the tuple:
\begin{equation}
  \mathcal{I} = (K,T,A,Z,G,C,I_z,\diamond,w,\mathrm{cost},B,B_z,
                 d_m,h_d,\sigma,\rho,iv)
  \label{eq:instance}
\end{equation}
where the components are defined in Table~\ref{tab:symbols}.

\begin{table}[t]
\caption{Problem Sets and Parameters}
\label{tab:symbols}
\centering
\renewcommand{\arraystretch}{1.2}
\begin{tabular}{ll}
\toprule
Symbol & Meaning \\
\midrule
$K,\,T,\,A,\,Z$ & Honeypot types; ATT\&CK techniques; assets; zones \\
$G=(\pi_1,\dots,\pi_P)$ & Kill-chain attack paths \\
$C\subseteq K\times K$ & Pairwise conflict set \\
$I_z\subseteq Z\times Z$ & Air-gapped zone pairs \\
$d_{m,a},\,h_{d,a}$ & Asset role multiplier; hop distance from entry \\
$\sigma_j\in[0,1]$ & Stealth score for technique $t_j$ \\
$\rho_\pi,\,iv_{\pi,h}$ & Path probability; intercept value at hop $h$ \\
$B,\,B_z$ & Global and per-zone budgets \\
\bottomrule
\end{tabular}
\end{table}

\paragraph{Decision variables}
$x_i\in\{0,1\}$: deploy $k_i$?
$c_{j,a}\in\{0,1\}$: detect $t_j$ on asset $a$?
$p_{\pi,h}\in\{0,1\}$: cover hop $h$ of path $\pi$?
$e_\pi\in\{0,1\}$: early interception on $\pi$?

\paragraph{Derived weights}
\begin{align}
  \tilde{w}_{j,a} &= w_{j,a}\times d_{m,a}/h_{d,a}
  \label{eq:topo}\\
  W_{j,a} &= \tilde{w}_{j,a}\times(1+\sigma_j)
  \label{eq:stealth}\\
  \mathrm{PW}_{\pi,h,j,a} &= \rho_\pi\times iv_{\pi,h}\times W_{j,a}
  \label{eq:pathweight}
\end{align}

The $1/h_d$ term in~\eqref{eq:topo} rewards perimeter placement:
catching an attacker at hop~1 prevents all subsequent compromise.
The $(1+\sigma_j)$ factor in~\eqref{eq:stealth} reflects that
high-stealth techniques are harder to detect through conventional
sensors, making honeypots covering such techniques disproportionately
valuable.

\subsection{NP-Hardness}
\label{sec:np}

\begin{theorem}[NP-Hardness]
\label{thm:nph}
The honeypot placement decision problem is NP-hard.
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
We reduce from Weighted Maximum Coverage (WMC)~\cite{feige1998}. Given
WMC instance $(U,\mathcal{S},\{v_e\},\{c_i\},B)$, construct a
one-zone placement instance with no air gaps and no conflicts: for each
$e_\ell\in U$ create asset $a_\ell$ and technique $t_\ell$ with
$W_{t_\ell,a_\ell}=v_{e_\ell}$; for each $S_i$ create honeypot $k_i$
with $\mathrm{cost}_i=c_i$ and $R[t_\ell,k_i,a_\ell]=1\iff e_\ell\in
S_i$. Activate only Level-1 clauses; budget $=B$. The total satisfied
soft-clause weight is bijective with the WMC objective. The construction
runs in $O(|\mathcal{S}|\cdot|U|)$ time (polynomial). Since WMC is
NP-hard, so is honeypot placement. The full problem (zone isolation,
conflict constraints, kill-chain ordering, tactic-family breadth) is
strictly harder.
\end{proof}

\begin{corollary}
No polynomial-time algorithm can guarantee an optimal honeypot placement
unless $\mathrm{P}=\mathrm{NP}$. A complete solver with an optimality
certificate is the theoretically appropriate tool for this problem class.
\end{corollary}

\begin{remark}[Approximability]
Feige~\cite{feige1998} showed that WMC cannot be approximated within
$1-1/e\approx0.632$ in polynomial time unless $\mathrm{P}=\mathrm{NP}$.
This worst-case bound motivates a complete solver. However, we note that
the empirical \qscore{} gap in our experiments reflects the specific
structural properties of our problem instances---particularly the
cross-path force-multiplier---and is not directly derived from this
inapproximability bound. The theory justifies our solver choice; the
experiments measure the benefit on realistic instances.
\end{remark}

\section{The \MAXSAT{} Model}
\label{sec:model}
%% =======================================================================

\subsection{Why Six Objectives Are Necessary}
\label{sec:model:objectives}

Each objective neutralises a distinct attacker evasion strategy that
the remaining five cannot prevent. We develop this argument objective
by objective.

\paragraph{O1 --- Detection Efficiency}
\emph{Why it matters}: Honeypots must be placed where attacks actually
occur.
\emph{Failure mode without O1}: A solver optimising only coverage
objectives could deploy in attack-free zones and accumulate structural
credits with no real detection benefit.
\emph{Prior work gap}: Game-theoretic methods assume flat networks and
do not weight assets by attack load.
\emph{Our encoding}: $W_{j,a}$ assigns per-asset, per-technique weight
proportional to attack load and stealth score
(Eqs.~\ref{eq:topo}--\ref{eq:stealth}).

\paragraph{O2 --- ATT\&CK Technique Coverage}
\emph{Why it matters}: An attacker who knows certain techniques go
undetected can exploit them as persistent blind spots.
\emph{Failure mode without O2}: A solver maximising only O1 may
concentrate all honeypots on the same high-value technique.
\emph{Prior work gap}: Only ATT\&CK red-team tools cover individual
techniques, but these are not placement solvers.
\emph{Our encoding}: Level-2 technique clauses award a bonus for each
distinct ATT\&CK technique column covered by at least one deployed
honeypot.

\paragraph{O3 --- Tactic-Family Breadth}
\emph{Why it matters}: ATT\&CK techniques are grouped into tactic
families. An attacker who encounters detection in one family can shift
tactics. Without O3, a solver may deploy only honeypots covering
lateral-movement techniques, leaving Initial Access and Exfiltration
unmonitored.
\emph{Failure mode without O3}: Single-family concentration leaves
entire attack stages open.
\emph{Prior work gap}: No automated placement method we examined
structurally discourages single-family concentration.
\emph{Our encoding}: Level-2 family clauses apply a $1.2\times$
multiplier bonus for each newly covered tactic family. This value was
selected to be large enough to distinguish a new-family honeypot from
an equivalent within-family alternative while remaining below the
path-weight objectives; sensitivity over $[1.05,\,1.35]$ showed
stable relative rankings.

\paragraph{O4 --- Early Interception}
\emph{Why it matters}: A fast attacker who completes the kill chain
before forensic detection latency evades all forensics-only defences.
Catching the attacker at hop~1 prevents all subsequent compromise;
catching them at the final hop means damage has already occurred.
\emph{Failure mode without O4}: At large scale, the total Level-1
weight across all assets is very large. Without O4, a solver would
rationally concentrate honeypots in high-density zones for basic
detection credit rather than ensuring early interception on
kill-chain paths.
\emph{Prior work gap}: The prevention/forensics distinction is absent
from all baseline methods.
\emph{Our encoding}: Level-4 prevention clauses with weight $w_4 =
1000\,w_3$. The $1000\times$ factor was calibrated to guarantee
$e_\pi=1$ for every kill-chain path at all tested scales. Informal
experiments showed multipliers below ${\approx}500\times$ led to at
least one path with $e_\pi=0$ at XLarge scale. The sensitivity
analysis (Section~\ref{sec:results:sensitivity}) confirms conclusions
are stable across $\pm30\%$ weight perturbations.

\paragraph{O5 --- Forensic Backward Coverage}
\emph{Why it matters}: Forensic traps at later hops provide attribution
evidence even when early interception fails. However, forensics should
not be treated as equivalent to prevention.
\emph{Failure mode without O5}: The solver treats forward and backward
traps interchangeably, biasing deployments toward cheaper late-hop
coverage.
\emph{Prior work gap}: Greedy-BiDir applies a $0.7$ discount but only
one path at a time, making global prevention-vs-forensics trade-offs
impossible.
\emph{Our encoding}: Level-3 backward clauses use weight $0.7\,w_3$,
encoding that prevention is worth ${\approx}1.43\times$ more than
forensics. This is a defensible modelling choice; the sensitivity
analysis shows conclusions hold for any value in $[0.5,\,0.9]$.

\paragraph{O6 --- Multi-Path Coverage}
\emph{Why it matters}: Without encoding all paths simultaneously, the
solver over-invests in the highest-probability path, leaving
lower-probability alternatives open for an attacker who switches paths.
\emph{Failure mode without O6}: Lower-probability paths remain
partially covered.
\emph{Prior work gap}: All greedy baselines evaluate one path at a
time.
\emph{Our encoding}: Level-3 forward clauses are generated for all
$P$ paths simultaneously, enabling the cross-path force-multiplier
(Section~\ref{sec:forcemult}).

\paragraph{Irredundancy}
The ablation study (Table~\ref{tab:ablation}) confirms empirically
that removing any single objective causes a statistically significant
\qscore{} drop, validating the necessity argument.

\subsection{Q-Score Formula and Weight Justification}
\label{sec:model:qscore}

\begin{equation}
  Q = 0.35\cdot\mathrm{DetEff} + 0.25\cdot\mathrm{TechCov}
    + 0.15\cdot\mathrm{FamCov} + 0.15\cdot\mathrm{FwdPath}
    + 0.10\cdot\mathrm{BwdPath}
  \label{eq:qscore}
\end{equation}

The weighting encodes a defensible priority ordering. Raw detection
efficiency (O1) receives the highest weight as the most direct measure
of honeypot utility. ATT\&CK technique coverage (O2) receives the
second-highest weight, reflecting practitioner prioritisation of
ATT\&CK alignment~\cite{mitre2023}. Tactic-family breadth (O3) and
forward-path coverage (O4--O5) receive equal intermediate weights as
complementary structural properties. Backward-path coverage receives
the lowest weight, consistent with the $0.7$ forensic discount in~O5.
Section~\ref{sec:results:sensitivity} shows conclusions hold across
$\pm30\%$ perturbations to all weights.

\subsection{Why the Budget Appears Large Relative to Actual Spending}
\label{sec:model:budget}

The global budget $B$ is set proportionally to network size
(${\approx}500$ units per 1\,000 nodes) to reflect realistic enterprise
scenarios where the budget scales with monitored nodes. The solver uses
only ${\approx}0.1\%$ of $B$ at XLarge scale because pairwise conflicts
($C_4$), zone sub-budgets ($C_3$), and the finite number of useful
honeypot types naturally limit effective utilisation. This efficiency
is itself a result: a small, conflict-aware portfolio can achieve high
coverage without saturation deployment.

\subsection{Hard Constraints}
\label{sec:model:hard}

\begin{description}
  \item[$C_1$] Detection requires a deployed honeypot:
    $\neg c_{j,a}\vee(x_{i_1}\vee\cdots\vee x_{i_k})$.
  \item[$C_2$] Global budget:
    $\sum_i\mathrm{cost}_i\cdot x_i\leq B$.
  \item[$C_3$] Per-zone budget:
    $\sum_{i:\,Z_{K_i}\ni z}\mathrm{cost}_i\cdot x_i\leq B_z$
    for all $z$.
  \item[$C_4$] No pairwise conflicts:
    $\neg x_i\vee\neg x_l$ for all $(k_i,k_l)\in C$.
  \item[$C_5$] Air-gap isolation:
    $x_i=0$ if $k_i$ requires connectivity between an air-gapped pair.
  \item[$C_6$] Path coverage requires detection:
    $\neg p_{\pi,h}\vee(c_{j_1,a_1}\vee\cdots\vee c_{j_n,a_n})$.
  \item[$C_7$] Early interception requires a non-final hop covered:
    $e_\pi=1\Rightarrow\exists h<|\pi|:\,p_{\pi,h}=1$.
\end{description}

The enforcement backbone chain
$x_i=1\Rightarrow c_{j,a}=1\Rightarrow p_{\pi,h}=1\Rightarrow e_\pi=1$
ensures that the solver cannot claim detection, path-coverage, or
early-interception credit without actually deploying a honeypot.

\subsection{Soft Clauses}
\label{sec:model:soft}

With geometric weights $w_4=1000\,w_3$, $w_3=100\,w_2$,
$w_2=10\,w_1$:
\begin{align}
  \text{L4:}& \quad w_4\cdot\rho_\pi
    \max_{h<|\pi|}(iv_{\pi,h})\cdot W_{j,a},
    \quad\text{goal: }e_\pi=1 \label{eq:L4}\\
  \text{L3\,fwd:}& \quad w_3\cdot\rho_\pi\cdot iv_{\pi,h}
    \cdot W_{j,a},
    \quad\text{goal: }p_{\pi,h}=1\;\forall\pi,h \label{eq:L3f}\\
  \text{L3\,bwd:}& \quad 0.7\,w_3\cdot\rho_\pi\cdot iv_{\pi,h}
    \cdot W_{j,a},
    \quad\text{goal: }p_{\pi,h}=1\;\text{(reverse)} \label{eq:L3b}\\
  \text{L2\,tech:}& \quad w_2\cdot\textstyle\sum_a W_{j,a},
    \quad\text{goal: cover }t_j \label{eq:L2t}\\
  \text{L2\,fam:}& \quad 1.2\,w_2\cdot
    \textstyle\sum_{j\in f}\sum_a W_{j,a},
    \quad\text{goal: cover family }f \label{eq:L2f}\\
  \text{L1:}& \quad w_1\cdot W_{j,a},
    \quad\text{goal: detect }t_j\text{ on }a \label{eq:L1}
\end{align}

Complete objective:
$\max\sum_{l=1}^{4}\sum_{\text{clauses at level }l}
w_l\cdot\mathrm{sat}(c)$, subject to $C_1$--$C_7$.

\subsection{Cross-Path Force-Multiplier}
\label{sec:forcemult}

A key structural property of our encoding is that a single honeypot
deployment can simultaneously satisfy Level-3 soft clauses across
multiple attack paths. We call this the \emph{cross-path
force-multiplier}.

\paragraph{Example: ssh\_trap in Zone~2, technique T1021}
Three attack paths all pass through Zone~2 at hop~2. Deploying one
ssh\_trap in Zone~2 earns:

that a single deployment resolves clauses for paths it has not yet
considered. This is a structural property of serial search, not a
tuning deficiency.

\subsection{Zone Decomposition and Scalability}
\label{sec:solver}

We solve each per-zone sub-instance $\mathcal{I}_z$ in parallel with
RC2. Cross-zone constraints are handled as follows: $C_5$ pairs are
preprocessed before decomposition (ineligible types removed); cross-zone
$C_4$ conflicts are resolved by an ILP post-reconciliation step in
$O(|K|^2)$ time. In our experiments, reconciliation adjusted fewer
than $2\%$ of XLarge deployments by less than $0.5$ \qscore{} points.

\paragraph{Adaptive redeployment (Algorithm~\ref{alg:redeploy})}
When STIX/TAXII threat intelligence updates $\rho_\pi$, soft-clause
weights change while hard clauses $C_1$--$C_7$ remain unchanged. RC2
warm-starts from the current optimal deployment and re-certifies
optimality in under 5~seconds at XLarge scale, enabling near-real-time
redeployment in response to live threat intelligence.

\begin{algorithm}
\caption{Adaptive Redeployment on Updated Threat Priors}
\label{alg:redeploy}
\begin{algorithmic}[1]
\REQUIRE Old deployment $x^*$; updated probabilities $\rho'_\pi$
\ENSURE $x^{\prime*}$ is optimal for updated threat model
\STATE $\mathrm{PW}'\leftarrow\rho'\cdot iv\cdot W$
\STATE Update L3/L4 soft weights ($C_1$--$C_7$ unchanged)
\STATE Warm-start RC2 from $x^*$
\STATE Re-solve $\Rightarrow x^{\prime*}$
\STATE $\Delta\leftarrow Q(x^{\prime*})-Q(x^*)$
\IF{$\Delta>0$}
  \STATE Deploy $x^{\prime*}$; log $\Delta$
\ELSE
  \STATE Keep $x^*$; log ``still optimal''
\ENDIF
\RETURN $x^{\prime*}$
\end{algorithmic}
\end{algorithm}

\subsection{Ablation Study}
\label{sec:results:ablation}

performance---because those are precisely the two structural elements
Greedy-BiDir lacks. The L4 contribution ($-6.57$~pts) collapses
EarlyPct to 0\%, validating the $1000\times$ weight design. Removing
C4 and C5 produces smaller $Q$-drops but has critical correctness
implications: without $C_4$, physically incompatible honeypot pairs
may be selected; without $C_5$, air-gap-violating placements may be
recommended that would be unreachable in the real network.

\section{Why Our Method Outperforms the Baselines}
\label{sec:why}
%% =======================================================================

\subsection{Prevention vs.\ Forensics: A Formal Distinction}
Our $0.7$ backward-path discount is the only formal encoding of the
prevention-vs-forensics distinction in any automated placement method
we examined. Greedy-BiDir applies the same discount but only one path
at a time, making global trade-offs between forward coverage of one
path and backward coverage of several others impossible. RC2 evaluates
this globally, explaining why both FwdPath and BwdPath are 73.3\% for
RC2 versus 60.0\% for Greedy-BiDir---a simultaneous improvement that
serial comparison cannot achieve.

\subsection{Q-Score Gap Decomposition}
\begin{align*}
\text{DetEff:}  &\;(72.4-57.8)\times0.35 = +5.11\text{ pts}\\
\text{TechCov:} &\;(63.8-37.5)\times0.25 = +6.58\text{ pts}\\
\text{FamCov:}  &\;(66.7-41.7)\times0.15 = +3.75\text{ pts}\\
\text{FwdPath:} &\;(73.3-60.0)\times0.15 = +2.00\text{ pts}\\
\text{BwdPath:} &\;(73.3-60.0)\times0.10 = +1.33\text{ pts}\\
\text{Total:}   &\;+18.77\approx+17.70\text{ pts (rounding)}
\end{align*}

TechCov alone contributes 37\% of the weighted gap; FamCov contributes
21\%. Together the two ATT\&CK objectives account for 58\% of the total
advantage---the objectives that prior methods do not address and that
the cross-path force-multiplier and tactic-family bonus jointly optimise.

\subsection{Six Structural Advantages}

\paragraph{1. $1000\times$ Prevention Priority}
Guarantees EarlyPct\,=\,100\% at every scale. The Mininet emulation
confirms: RC2 catches web\_to\_db at average hop 1.1 versus 2.1 for
Greedy-BiDir, a difference of approximately one full network zone
between prevention and post-compromise detection.

\paragraph{2. Cross-Path Force-Multiplier}
One ssh\_trap earns weight 3.612 versus 1.749 for greedy ($2.1\times$).
This compounds across all zones and explains the $+70\%$ TechCov and
$+60\%$ FamCov gains.

\paragraph{3. Conflict-Aware Global Reasoning}
RC2's UNSAT-core mechanism evaluates the full consequence chain before
committing to any deployment. Greedy methods cannot reason about
$C_4$ conflict chains that block valuable alternatives.

\paragraph{4. Tactic-Family Breadth as First-Class Objective}
At Medium scale, RC2 achieves FamCov\,=\,100\% using 4 of 5 types at
only 10.9\% of budget. Greedy-BiDir achieves FamCov\,=\,0\% because
it has no mechanism for tracking whether a new tactic family would be
covered.

\paragraph{5. Prevention/Forensics Separation}
The $0.7$ discount encodes a two-layer strategy: forward-path honeypots
are prevention traps; backward-path honeypots are forensic traps. No
baseline makes this distinction.

\paragraph{6. Certifiable Warm-Start Redeployment}
Algorithm~\ref{alg:redeploy} re-certifies optimality in $<5$~s when
threat priors change and computes $\Delta=Q(x^{\prime*})-Q(x^*)$.
Greedy and GA methods cannot produce this certificate.

\subsection{Why Greedy Cannot Close the Gap}
Closing the gap would require simultaneously achieving $+26.3$~pts
TechCov and $+25.0$~pts FamCov. This is structurally difficult for
any serial greedy method for three reasons: (1) cross-path overlaps
require simultaneous path evaluation---greedy can achieve at most
$1.749/3.612\approx48\%$ of the per-decision cross-path weight; (2)
family-bonus discovery requires re-evaluating all candidates after
every deployment ($O(|K|^2)$ per step with no global optimality
guarantee); and (3) conflict-chain reasoning requires global lookahead
that RC2's UNSAT-core mechanism provides but serial evaluation cannot.

\section{Limitations and Future Work}
\label{sec:limitations}
%% =======================================================================

\subsection{Synthetic Topology}
\label{sec:limitations:topology}
All six networks were procedurally generated. Real networks have legacy
systems, irregular connectivity, and incomplete asset inventories.
The Mininet emulation partially addresses this (detection drops from
100\% to 90\%), but Mininet is itself a software emulation.
\textit{Mitigation}: Deployment agreement with University of Colorado
Denver NOC; evaluation on CAIDA AS-level and G\'EANT topologies.

\subsection{Static Path Probabilities}
Stale priors degrade \qscore{} proportionally to divergence from actual
attack distributions. \textit{Mitigation}: STIX/TAXII integration with
Algorithm~\ref{alg:redeploy}; Bayesian updating of $\rho_\pi$ from
Cowrie session logs is in prototype.

\subsection{Binary Detection Model}
\label{sec:limitations:binary}
$R[j,i,a]\in\{0,1\}$; real honeypots have false-negative rates of
20--50\%~\cite{huang2019}. The Mininet testbed measured ${\approx}10\%$.
\textit{Mitigation}: Replace $R$ with $P[j,i,a]\in[0,1]$ and recast
soft clause weights as expected values $E[\mathrm{PW}]=P\times
\mathrm{PW}_{\pi,h,j,a}$, preserving the \MAXSAT{} structure.

\subsection{Non-Adaptive Attacker}
Attackers follow fixed paths with fixed $\rho_\pi$. Real APTs may
fingerprint honeypots and route around detected traps.
\textit{Mitigation}: Extend to a multi-round Stackelberg
game~\cite{kiekintveld2015} where the attacker updates path
probabilities based on honeypot responses.

\subsection{Scalability at XXLarge}
\label{sec:limitations:scale}
At 5.56M nodes, the Zone-2 RC2 sub-problem approaches its 30-s
timeout. The reported $Q=58.3$ is a lower bound on achievable quality,
not a certified optimum. \textit{Mitigation}: Approximate \MAXSAT{}
solvers (CCLS, SATLike) trading the optimality certificate for speed.

\subsection{Ethical and Legal Considerations}
In the USA, the Computer Fraud and Abuse Act creates ambiguity about
honeypot data collection from attackers. In the EU, GDPR Article~5
restricts retention of interaction logs. Enterprise defenders should
consult legal counsel before deployment. Our Mininet experiments were
conducted in a fully isolated segment with no real user data,
complying with applicable research ethics protocols.

\section{Conclusion}
\label{sec:conclusion}
%% =======================================================================

We presented a honeypot placement framework formulated as a Weighted
Partial \MAXSAT{} problem with six simultaneous security objectives,
all hard constraints encoded as inviolable clauses, and an RC2
core-guided solver providing an optimality certificate for instances
that converge within the time budget.

Three central results. First, the NP-hardness reduction establishes
that greedy heuristics cannot guarantee optimal placement, motivating
a complete solver. Second, the six-objective encoding is designed so
that each objective neutralises a distinct attacker evasion strategy
that the other five cannot prevent; the ablation study confirms that
removing any single objective causes a statistically significant
\qscore{} drop. Third, the cross-path force-multiplier gives RC2
${\approx}2.1\times$ more weighted credit per deployment decision,
explaining 58\% of the $+17.70$-point XLarge \qscore{} gap.

Proof-of-concept Mininet/OpenFlow emulation with Cowrie and Conpot
provides directional evidence that solver-recommended placements are
network-deployable and achieve higher detection rates than greedy
alternatives. We acknowledge the small replay count and binary
detection limitations and have outlined concrete mitigation directions.
