\begin{abstract}
Deciding where to place honeypots in a large enterprise network is a
hard combinatorial optimisation problem. Enterprise networks are
divided into security zones with different trust levels, contain
diverse server types, and face adversaries who follow structured
kill-chain attack patterns. Budget constraints, pairwise honeypot
conflicts, and physical air gaps jointly restrict the set of feasible
deployments, making ad-hoc or greedy placement strategies insufficient
for achieving comprehensive detection.

We model honeypot placement as a Weighted Partial Maximum
Satisfiability (\MAXSAT{}) problem with six simultaneous objectives:
\emph{detection efficiency}~(O1), \emph{ATT\&CK technique
coverage}~(O2), \emph{tactic-family breadth}~(O3),
\emph{early attacker interception}~(O4), \emph{forensic backward
coverage}~(O5), and \emph{multi-path coverage}~(O6). These six
objectives are the structural foundation of our framework: each
one addresses a distinct attacker evasion strategy that the other
five cannot prevent, and together they define the space of properties
a robust honeypot placement must satisfy. All hard constraints are
encoded as inviolable clauses. We establish NP-hardness via
polynomial-time reduction from Weighted Maximum Coverage and solve
with the RC2 core-guided solver, which provides an optimality
certificate for instances that converge within the time budget.

We evaluate across six synthetic network scales (100 to 5.56~million
nodes) and conduct proof-of-concept emulation on a five-zone
Mininet/OpenFlow testbed running real Cowrie SSH and Conpot ICS
honeypots. At the 500\,000-node scale our solver achieves $Q=71.39$
versus $Q=53.69$ for the strongest baseline ($+17.70$~pts,
$+32.9\%$). Emulation confirms that solver placements are deployable
and directionally superior to greedy alternatives, subject to the
limitations discussed in Section~\ref{sec:limitations}.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:intro}
%% =======================================================================

\subsection{The Budget-Constrained Placement Problem}
\label{sec:intro:budget}

A honeypot is a deliberately exposed computing resource whose sole
purpose is to attract adversaries. Because no legitimate user should
interact with it, any connection is a strong indicator of malicious
activity. When placed strategically, honeypots can detect attackers
early in the kill chain, expose their techniques, and buy defenders
time to respond before critical assets are compromised. When placed
poorly, they consume scarce security budget and produce no actionable
intelligence.

The gap between good and poor placement is not marginal. A network
operator with a fixed budget must decide not only \emph{how many}
honeypots to deploy but \emph{which types} to use and \emph{where}
to position them across a heterogeneous enterprise topology. These
decisions interact non-trivially: a single SSH honeypot at the
perimeter may be worth more than a dozen generic traps deep in a
low-traffic subnet, not because it covers more nodes, but because
it intercepts attackers before they reach sensitive systems. No
operational heuristic reliably identifies the best combination when
hundreds of server types, thousands of network nodes, and multiple
concurrent attack paths must be considered simultaneously.

The budget constraint makes this more than a coverage problem. A
deployment that exhausts the budget on redundant honeypot types
covering the same techniques leaves other attack stages completely
unmonitored. A deployment that spreads thinly across many types may
satisfy zone coverage requirements while failing to intercept any
specific kill-chain path early enough to prevent damage. What is
needed is a principled framework that treats budget efficiency,
detection diversity, and kill-chain awareness as simultaneous
requirements---not sequential trade-offs.

\subsection{Enterprise Zones and Air Gaps Create Structural Complexity}
\label{sec:intro:zones}

Real enterprise networks are not flat. They are partitioned into
security zones---DMZ, internal LAN, cloud services, OT/SCADA
systems, and management infrastructure---each with distinct trust
levels, permitted traffic flows, and asset compositions. This
structure imposes three kinds of constraint on honeypot placement.

First, \emph{air-gap constraints}: OT and management zones are
physically or logically isolated from less-trusted zones, making
certain honeypot types unreachable from specific zones regardless
of cost. A honeypot type that requires SSH connectivity cannot be
deployed in an air-gapped OT zone, even if it would otherwise be
the most valuable choice.

Second, \emph{pairwise conflicts}: some honeypot types produce
redundant signals or share monitoring resources in ways that make
deploying both simultaneously worse than deploying either alone. For
example, deploying both a generic trap and a DNS trap in the same
subnet may saturate the monitoring pipeline without proportional
coverage gain. These conflicts must be explicitly encoded as
hard constraints---they cannot be approximated away.

Third, \emph{per-zone budget sub-limits}: the total monitoring
capacity of each zone constrains how much can be deployed there,
independent of the global budget.

Together, air gaps, conflicts, and zone sub-budgets create a
combinatorially complex feasibility space that is incompatible with
greedy or rule-of-thumb placement strategies. As we show formally
in Section~\ref{sec:np}, the resulting decision problem is NP-hard.

\subsection{Attacker Kill Chains Demand Structured Objectives}
\label{sec:intro:killchain}

Sophisticated adversaries do not behave randomly. They follow
structured kill chains~\cite{hutchins2011} and employ techniques
catalogued in the MITRE ATT\&CK framework~\cite{mitre2023}, moving
systematically from initial access through lateral movement to their
final objective. This structure has profound implications for honeypot
placement that go beyond simple coverage maximisation.

\textbf{The prevention--forensics distinction.}
A honeypot that catches an attacker at hop~1 prevents all subsequent
compromise. A honeypot that catches them at the final hop only
records what has already been done---data may already be exfiltrated,
systems already corrupted. From an operational standpoint, these two
outcomes carry very different security value. A placement framework
that treats them as equivalent will systematically favour cheaper
forensic traps over prevention traps, since forensic traps often
cover more hops per unit cost. This preference must be encoded
explicitly in the objective, not applied as a post-hoc adjustment.

\textbf{The multi-path coverage problem.}
Attackers do not always follow the most probable route to their
target. A resilient placement must cover diverse ATT\&CK techniques
and multiple concurrent kill-chain paths simultaneously. A solver
that optimises one path at a time---as all greedy baselines do---will
over-invest in the highest-probability route and leave alternative
paths open for an attacker who switches tactics or entry points.

\textbf{The tactic-family evasion problem.}
ATT\&CK techniques are grouped into tactic families (Initial Access,
Lateral Movement, Exfiltration, etc.). An attacker who encounters a
honeypot covering lateral-movement techniques can shift to an Initial
Access technique that goes unmonitored. A placement that concentrates
coverage in one tactic family leaves the attacker with a systematic
evasion route. Among the automated placement methods we examined,
none includes a mechanism that structurally discourages single-family
concentration.

\subsection{Why Six Objectives, and Why All Six Are Necessary}
\label{sec:intro:whysix}

The three problems identified above---budget efficiency, kill-chain
structure, and multi-path coverage---cannot be addressed by
optimising a single metric. They require six distinct objectives
that together form an \emph{irredundant covering set} over the space
of attacker evasion strategies. We introduce each objective here and
develop the full technical argument in Section~\ref{sec:model:objectives}.

\begin{description}[leftmargin=!, labelwidth=\widthof{\textbf{O6.}}]
  \item[\textbf{O1.}] \textbf{Detection Efficiency}: cover
    high-value assets in zones with high attack load. Without O1,
    the solver may deploy in attack-free zones.
  \item[\textbf{O2.}] \textbf{ATT\&CK Technique Coverage}: cover
    diverse techniques across the ATT\&CK matrix. Without O2, an
    attacker exploiting uncovered techniques goes undetected.
  \item[\textbf{O3.}] \textbf{Tactic-Family Breadth}: cover multiple
    ATT\&CK tactic families. Without O3, single-family concentration
    leaves entire attack stages unmonitored.
  \item[\textbf{O4.}] \textbf{Early Interception}: structurally prefer
    prevention over forensics. Without O4, fast-moving attackers
    complete the kill chain before detection fires.
  \item[\textbf{O5.}] \textbf{Forensic Backward Coverage}: maintain a
    secondary forensic layer, explicitly discounted relative to
    prevention. Without O5, the solver conflates prevention and
    forensics and misprices both.
  \item[\textbf{O6.}] \textbf{Multi-Path Coverage}: encode all attack
    paths simultaneously. Without O6, lower-probability paths remain
    open as attacker escape routes.
\end{description}

We will show---formally in the NP-hardness proof and empirically in
the ablation study---that removing any single one of these objectives
leaves a distinct evasion channel that the remaining five cannot close.

\subsection{Our Approach: Weighted Partial \MAXSAT{}}
\label{sec:intro:approach}

We encode all six objectives and all hard constraints (budget, zone
isolation, pairwise conflicts, air gaps) as a single Weighted Partial
\MAXSAT{} instance. Soft clauses capture objectives; hard clauses
enforce constraints that cannot be violated under any circumstances.
The RC2 core-guided solver~\cite{rc2} finds a placement that
maximises total satisfied soft-clause weight, providing an optimality
certificate for instances that converge within the per-zone time
budget. For the largest scale tested (5.56M nodes), the zone-2
sub-problem may not fully converge before timeout; results at that
scale are reported as lower bounds on achievable quality rather than
certified optima (Section~\ref{sec:limitations:scale}).

\subsection{Contributions}
\label{sec:intro:contrib}

\begin{enumerate}
  \item \textbf{Six-objective \MAXSAT{} encoding}: We formalise each
    of O1--O6 as a family of weighted soft clauses, justify each
    clause weight on principled grounds, and show why each objective
    is necessary (Section~\ref{sec:model}).

  \item \textbf{NP-hardness proof}: We establish that the placement
    decision problem is NP-hard via polynomial-time reduction from
    Weighted Maximum Coverage~\cite{feige1998}
    (Section~\ref{sec:np}).

  \item \textbf{Cross-path force-multiplier analysis}: We show that
    RC2's UNSAT-core mechanism simultaneously resolves soft clauses
    across multiple attack paths in a single decision step, yielding
    approximately $2.0$--$2.2\times$ more weighted credit per honeypot
    deployment than the greedy baselines on the same instances
    (Section~\ref{sec:forcemult}).

  \item \textbf{Zone-decomposed scalable solver}: We decompose the
    problem by zone, enabling parallel RC2 instances that scale to
    5.56M nodes in approximately 30~seconds
    (Section~\ref{sec:solver}).

  \item \textbf{Proof-of-concept emulation}: We deploy
    solver-recommended placements (Cowrie SSH and Conpot ICS
    honeypots) in a five-zone Mininet/OpenFlow testbed and replay
    structured attack sequences. The emulation provides directional
    evidence of deployability and detection improvement; it is not
    a production deployment, and its results should be interpreted
    accordingly (Section~\ref{sec:emulation}).
\end{enumerate}

\section{Related Work}
\label{sec:related}
%% =======================================================================

We organise prior work around five capability dimensions:
\textbf{D1}~multi-zone topology with air gaps, \textbf{D2}~kill-chain
ordering with early-interception preference, \textbf{D3}~ATT\&CK
integration as live solver objectives, \textbf{D4}~budget and
conflict hard constraints, and \textbf{D5}~a proven optimality
certificate. Table~\ref{tab:capability} maps each prior method
against these five dimensions and shows that no existing approach
satisfies all five simultaneously. Our survey covers honeypot
placement literature indexed in IEEE Xplore, ACM Digital Library,
and USENIX proceedings from 2000 to 2024, supplemented by forward
citation tracking from the foundational
works~\cite{provos2004,spitzner2003,kiekintveld2015}.
Table~\ref{tab:related_obj} provides a finer-grained mapping of each
method against our six security objectives (O1--O6), clarifying
which specific objective gaps each prior method leaves open.

\paragraph{Foundational honeypot work}
Provos~\cite{provos2004} and Spitzner~\cite{spitzner2003} established
the conceptual foundations of honeynets but relied on manual expert
placement without automation, budget modelling, or kill-chain
awareness. Yuill et al.~\cite{yuill2004} extended the concept to
honeyfiles for file-level intrusion detection. Wagener et
al.~\cite{wagener2009} proposed adaptive self-configuring honeypots,
and Fraunholz et al.~\cite{fraunholz2017} characterised specific
deployment challenges in industrial control systems. These works
establish the operational value of honeypots but do not address the
automated multi-zone placement problem under budget and conflict
constraints.

\paragraph{Game-theoretic deception frameworks}
Kiekintveld et al.~\cite{kiekintveld2015} showed that mixed (randomised)
strategies dominate deterministic placement against rational adversaries
in a Stackelberg game. Pawlick et al.~\cite{pawlick2019} provided a
comprehensive taxonomy of defensive deception strategies and their
equilibrium properties. Wang et al.~\cite{wang2018} surveyed cyber
deception and identified honeypot placement as a key open research
problem. Achleitner et al.~\cite{achleitner2017} used virtual networks
to defend against insider reconnaissance via deception. Tan et
al.~\cite{tan2011} explored network-layer obliviousness as a
complementary deception primitive. All of these works assume flat or
homogeneous networks; none address multi-zone topology (D1),
kill-chain ordering (D2), ATT\&CK-based objectives (D3), or
pairwise conflict constraints (D4).

\paragraph{Reinforcement learning for honeypot engagement}
Huang and Zhu~\cite{huang2019} applied semi-Markov decision process
RL to learn adaptive engagement policies---deciding \emph{how} to
interact with an attacker once they have entered a honeypot. Their
problem is complementary to ours: we decide \emph{where} to deploy
honeypots; their RL decides \emph{how to engage} once an attacker
arrives. The two approaches can be composed naturally.

\paragraph{ATT\&CK-based automated defence}
Applebaum et al.~\cite{applebaum2016} used ATT\&CK for automated
red-team emulation, producing realistic attacker behaviour models but
no placement algorithm. Hemberg et al.~\cite{hemberg2018} applied a
genetic algorithm (GA) to ATT\&CK-based defensive control selection,
partially addressing D3 but evaluating one objective at a time. A
GA that evaluates objectives sequentially cannot discover the
cross-path synergies that require global simultaneous optimisation.

\paragraph{Formal methods and optimal sensor placement}
Saha and Gera~\cite{saha2015} formulated sensor placement as an ILP
and achieved proven-optimal placements satisfying budget constraints
(D4, D5), but without kill-chain ordering (D2), ATT\&CK objectives
(D3), or scalability beyond approximately 50\,000 nodes.
Pinisetty et al.~\cite{pinisetty2017} applied \MAXSAT{} to runtime
enforcement in cyber-physical systems, establishing the feasibility
of formal security-constraint encoding; their formulation does not
model zone structure, kill chains, or ATT\&CK coverage.
Liffiton and Malik~\cite{liffiton2016} developed efficient MUS
enumeration that underlies RC2's core-guided search mechanism.
Jajodia et al.~\cite{jajodia2005} analysed chokepoints in attack
graphs, providing intuition that informs our path-weight formulation,
but offer no automated placement algorithm.

\paragraph{Policy and access-control formal methods}
Al-Shaer and Hamed~\cite{alshaer2004}, Bak et al.~\cite{bak2009},
and Fisler et al.~\cite{fisler2005} developed formal specification
and verification tools for firewall and access-control policies. These
share our motivation for rigorous constraint encoding but address
policy correctness rather than deceptive sensor placement.
Bozorgi et al.~\cite{bozorgi2010} applied machine learning to
vulnerability classification, informing the technique stealth scores
$\sigma_j$ used in our weight formulation.

\paragraph{Five Capability Dimensions}
To evaluate prior work systematically, we define five capability
dimensions that a complete automated honeypot placement framework
must satisfy. Each dimension directly motivates one or more of the
six security objectives (O1--O6) introduced in
Section~\ref{sec:intro:whysix}:

\begin{description}[leftmargin=2em, itemsep=2pt]
  \item[\textbf{D1 --- Multi-zone topology with air gaps.}]
    Enterprise networks contain zones with different trust levels
    separated by firewalls and physical air gaps. A placement framework
    that assumes a flat network cannot enforce zone-specific constraints
    or model air-gap isolation. \emph{This dimension motivates O1
    (zone-aware detection efficiency) and the hard constraints
    $C_3$--$C_5$ (per-zone budgets and air-gap isolation).}

  \item[\textbf{D2 --- Kill-chain ordering with early interception.}]
    Attackers follow structured paths through the network. A placement
    that ignores hop order treats a hop-1 interception (prevention)
    equivalently to a final-hop catch (forensics), systematically
    mis-valuing sensor positions. \emph{This dimension motivates O4
    (early interception, $1000\times$ Level-4 weight) and O5 (forensic
    backward coverage, $0.7$ discount), encoded in soft clauses L4
    and L3-bwd.}

  \item[\textbf{D3 --- ATT\&CK integration as live solver objectives.}]
    The MITRE ATT\&CK framework catalogues adversary techniques and
    tactic families. A solver that does not treat ATT\&CK coverage as
    a direct optimisation objective cannot guarantee technique-breadth
    or tactic-family diversity in its placements. \emph{This dimension
    motivates O2 (technique coverage, Level-2 technique clauses) and
    O3 (tactic-family breadth, $1.2\times$ family bonus).}

  \item[\textbf{D4 --- Budget and conflict hard constraints.}]
    Real deployments face a global budget ceiling, per-zone spending
    limits, and pairwise honeypot conflicts (redundant signals, shared
    monitoring resources). These must be encoded as inviolable hard
    constraints, not soft preferences. \emph{This dimension motivates
    constraints $C_1$--$C_4$ and directly enables the budget-efficient
    portfolios demonstrated in Section~\ref{sec:results:xxlarge}.}

  \item[\textbf{D5 --- Proven optimality certificate.}]
    A heuristic or greedy method cannot certify that no better
    placement exists within the constraint set. The NP-hardness of
    the placement problem (Theorem~\ref{thm:nph}) means that only a
    complete solver can provide such a certificate. \emph{This
    dimension motivates the use of the RC2 core-guided \MAXSAT{}
    solver and the zone-decomposed parallel architecture of
    Section~\ref{sec:solver}.}
\end{description}

fully satisfied row.

\end{equation}
where the components are defined in Table~\ref{tab:symbols}.

\paragraph{Decision variables}
$x_i\in\{0,1\}$: deploy honeypot type $k_i$?
$c_{j,a}\in\{0,1\}$: detect technique $t_j$ on asset $a$?
$p_{\pi,h}\in\{0,1\}$: cover hop $h$ of path $\pi$?
$e_\pi\in\{0,1\}$: early interception on $\pi$?

\paragraph{Derived weights}
Before solving, we compute topology-adjusted and stealth-adjusted
weights:
\begin{align}
  \tilde{w}_{j,a} &= w_{j,a}\times d_{m,a}/h_{d,a}
  \label{eq:topo}\\
  W_{j,a} &= \tilde{w}_{j,a}\times(1+\sigma_j)
  \label{eq:stealth}\\
  \mathrm{PW}_{\pi,h,j,a} &= \rho_\pi\times iv_{\pi,h}\times W_{j,a}
  \label{eq:pathweight}
\end{align}

The $1/h_d$ term in~\eqref{eq:topo} encodes the key security
intuition that perimeter placement is preferred: catching an attacker
at hop~1 prevents all subsequent hops and therefore all downstream
damage. The $(1+\sigma_j)$ factor in~\eqref{eq:stealth} reflects
that high-stealth techniques---those which blend into normal
traffic---are harder to detect through conventional monitoring; a
honeypot covering such a technique is therefore disproportionately
valuable relative to its cost.

\subsection{NP-Hardness}
\label{sec:np}

\begin{theorem}[NP-Hardness]
\label{thm:nph}
The honeypot placement decision problem is NP-hard.
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
We reduce from Weighted Maximum Coverage (WMC)~\cite{feige1998}.
Given WMC instance $(U,\mathcal{S},\{v_e\},\{c_i\},B)$, construct a
one-zone placement instance with no air gaps and no conflicts: for
each $e_\ell\in U$ create asset $a_\ell$ and technique $t_\ell$ with
$W_{t_\ell,a_\ell}=v_{e_\ell}$; for each $S_i$ create honeypot $k_i$
with $\mathrm{cost}_i=c_i$ and $R[t_\ell,k_i,a_\ell]=1\iff e_\ell
\in S_i$. Activate only Level-1 (detection) clauses; set budget
$=B$. The total satisfied soft-clause weight is bijective with the
WMC objective. The construction runs in $O(|\mathcal{S}|\cdot|U|)$
time. Since WMC is NP-hard, so is honeypot placement. The full
problem---with zone isolation, conflict constraints, kill-chain
ordering, and tactic-family breadth---is strictly harder than this
one-zone reduction, and hence also NP-hard.
\end{proof}

\begin{corollary}
\label{cor:nopoly}
No polynomial-time algorithm can guarantee an optimal honeypot
placement unless $\mathrm{P}=\mathrm{NP}$. A complete solver that
provides an optimality certificate is the theoretically appropriate
tool for this problem class.
\end{corollary}

\begin{remark}[Approximability and the empirical gap]
\label{rem:approx}
Feige~\cite{feige1998} showed that WMC cannot be approximated
within $1-1/e\approx0.632$ in polynomial time unless
$\mathrm{P}=\mathrm{NP}$. This worst-case inapproximability result
provides theoretical motivation for a complete solver in place of
greedy heuristics. We emphasise, however, that the empirical
\qscore{} gap observed in our experiments
(Section~\ref{sec:results:xlarge}) is a consequence of specific
structural properties of our problem instances---particularly the
cross-path force-multiplier described in
Section~\ref{sec:forcemult}---and is not directly derived from this
worst-case bound. The inapproximability result establishes that
greedy methods cannot provide optimality guarantees; the experiments
quantify the benefit of a complete solver on realistic instances.
\end{remark}

\subsection{Why Each Objective Is Necessary: The Evasion-Space Argument}
\label{sec:model:objectives}

The six objectives are not a list of desirable features---they
are a covering set over the space of attacker evasion strategies.
We define this more precisely: each objective is \emph{necessary}
in the sense that removing it opens a distinct evasion channel that
the remaining five objectives cannot close. We argue this claim
objective by objective, and the ablation study (Table~\ref{tab:ablation})
provides independent empirical confirmation.

\paragraph{O1 --- Detection Efficiency}
\textit{Why it matters.}
A honeypot deployed in a zone with negligible attack activity provides
zero operational value regardless of its technique coverage. Placing
resources where attacks actually occur is a prerequisite for any
useful detection.

\textit{Failure mode.}
A solver optimising only structural coverage objectives (O2--O6)
could place honeypots in low-traffic internal segments, accumulating
coverage credits on paper while leaving the most attack-exposed
zones unmonitored.

\textit{Prior work gap.}
Game-theoretic methods~\cite{kiekintveld2015,pawlick2019} assume flat
networks and do not model zone-specific attack loads. Sensor placement
ILP~\cite{saha2015} weights assets by type but not by observed attack
distribution.

\textit{Our encoding.}
We compute $W_{j,a}=\tilde{w}_{j,a}(1+\sigma_j)$ using attack-load
weights $w_{j,a}$ that reflect the probability of technique $t_j$
being used against asset $a$, adjusted for the asset's role
multiplier $d_{m,a}$ and hop distance $h_{d,a}$. Level-1 soft clauses
reward each $(t_j,a)$ pair detected, directing the solver toward
high-traffic zones.

\paragraph{O2 --- ATT\&CK Technique Coverage}
\textit{Why it matters.}
Attackers who know that certain ATT\&CK techniques go undetected will
preferentially exploit those techniques. A honeypot network with gaps
in its technique coverage offers a systematic blind spot that a
capable adversary will find.

\textit{Failure mode.}
A solver maximising O1 alone concentrates all honeypots on the same
few high-value techniques, potentially covering multiple assets with
the same technique while leaving many other techniques completely
unmonitored.

\textit{Prior work gap.}
ATT\&CK red-team tools~\cite{applebaum2016} model individual
techniques but are not placement solvers. The GA-based
approach~\cite{hemberg2018} partially covers D3 but evaluates
objectives one at a time and cannot enforce a global technique-breadth
requirement.

\textit{Our encoding.}
Level-2 technique clauses award a bonus equal to the total weighted
coverage $\sum_a W_{j,a}$ for each distinct technique $t_j$ that is
covered by at least one deployed honeypot. This creates direct
incentive to spread coverage across ATT\&CK technique columns, not
just across assets.

\paragraph{O3 --- Tactic-Family Breadth}
\textit{Why it matters.}
ATT\&CK techniques are grouped into tactic families (Initial Access,
Lateral Movement, Privilege Escalation, Exfiltration, etc.). An
attacker who encounters a honeypot covering one tactic family can
shift to a different family. Without O3, the solver may deploy all
honeypots covering lateral-movement techniques, leaving Initial
Access and Exfiltration entirely unmonitored. This is not a corner
case: it is the rational response to a solver that does not penalise
single-family concentration.

\textit{Failure mode.}
O1 and O2 together reward covering high-value techniques wherever
they occur. If the highest-value techniques all belong to one tactic
family, the solver will concentrate there, inadvertently leaving
other stages of the kill chain open.

\textit{Prior work gap.}
Among the automated placement methods we examined, none includes a
mechanism that structurally discourages single-family concentration.
The Greedy-BiDir baseline selects honeypots by value-per-dollar and
has no tactic-family awareness.

\textit{Our encoding and parameter justification.}
Level-2 family clauses apply a $1.2\times$ multiplier bonus for
each newly covered tactic family. The value $1.2$ was chosen to
satisfy two design requirements: (a) it must be large enough that,
when two honeypot types offer equal path-weight coverage, the solver
prefers the one covering a new family; (b) it must be small enough
not to override path-weight considerations in O4--O6. A value of
$1.0$ (no bonus) provides no diversity incentive. A value of $2.0$
or more causes the family bonus to dominate path-weight objectives,
producing family-diverse placements that miss high-probability paths.
Sensitivity analysis over $[1.05,\,1.35]$ confirmed stable relative
rankings (Section~\ref{sec:results:sensitivity}).

\paragraph{O4 --- Early Interception}
\textit{Why it matters.}
An attacker who completes the kill chain faster than the forensic
detection latency evades all defences. Catching the attacker at
hop~1 prevents all downstream damage. Catching them at the final hop
means the target asset has already been reached. This prevention--
forensics distinction is not a secondary concern---it is the most
operationally important property a honeypot network can have.

\textit{Failure mode.}
Without O4, the solver rationally maximises total detection weight.
At large scale (500k nodes), the aggregate Level-1 weight across
all assets and techniques is enormous. A solver without O4 would
concentrate honeypots in high-density zones (Zone~2 with 40\% of
assets) to maximise this aggregate, catching attackers at hop~2 of
a 3-hop path after they have already traversed the perimeter.

\textit{Prior work gap.}
The prevention--forensics distinction is absent from all baseline
methods. Greedy-BiDir applies a $0.7$ discount to backward-path
coverage, but this is a post-hoc adjustment applied one path at a
time---not a structural mechanism for ensuring at least one prevention
trap per kill-chain path.

\textit{Our encoding and parameter justification.}
Level-4 prevention clauses use weight $w_4=1000\,w_3$. The
$1000\times$ multiplier is motivated by the following order-of-magnitude
argument. At XLarge scale (500k nodes, 5 paths), the aggregate Level-3
forward weight the solver can earn across all paths and assets is
approximately $\sum_\pi \rho_\pi \cdot |A_\pi| \cdot \bar{W} \approx
(0.30{+}0.25{+}0.20{+}0.15{+}0.10) \times 10^4 \times 0.5 \approx
5{,}000\,w_3$, where $|A_\pi|$ is the number of path-relevant assets
and $\bar{W}\approx0.5$ is the average path-weight per asset. For the
Level-4 bonus to dominate---\ie, for the solver to always prefer
placing one honeypot on a non-final hop over accumulating additional
Level-3 path credits---the Level-4 weight per path must comfortably
exceed the maximum marginal Level-3 gain of a single deployment
(${\approx}3$--$5\,w_3$ per honeypot). A multiplier of $1000\times$
provides the required headroom by a factor of ${\approx}200$, ensuring
the dominance holds even after zone decomposition and budget
sub-limits reduce the effective path count. Empirically, multipliers
below approximately $500\times$ led to at least one path with
$e_\pi=0$ at XLarge scale, while $1000\times$ guaranteed $e_\pi=1$
for every path at every tested scale. We use $1000\times$ as a
conservative choice; the sensitivity analysis
(Section~\ref{sec:results:sensitivity}) confirms the advantage
persists across $\pm30\%$ perturbations to this weight.

\paragraph{O5 --- Forensic Backward Coverage}
\textit{Why it matters.}
Forensic traps at later hops provide attribution evidence and
support incident response even when early interception fails. They
should not be ignored. However, they must not be treated as
equivalent to prevention traps: an attacker caught at the final hop
has already reached the target asset, and the collected forensic
data cannot undo that damage.

\textit{Failure mode.}
Without an explicit discount, the solver has no mechanism to
distinguish a honeypot that prevents compromise from one that merely
records it. The resulting deployment will mix prevention and forensic
traps without structural preference, and may favour cheaper forensic
placements that fail to stop fast-moving attackers.

\textit{Prior work gap.}
Greedy-BiDir applies a $0.7$ discount but does so per path in
isolation. This prevents the global comparison---is forward coverage
of this path worth more than backward coverage of those three
paths?---that only simultaneous encoding can provide.

\textit{Our encoding and parameter justification.}
Level-3 backward clauses use weight $0.7\,w_3$. The value $0.7$
encodes the modelling assumption that forensics is worth approximately
70\% of prevention. This reflects the operational principle that
prevention---stopping the attacker before the target is reached---is
the primary security objective, while forensics is a secondary
value~\cite{hutchins2011}. The value $0.7$ creates a quantifiable
separation without so severely discounting forensic traps that the
solver ignores later-hop coverage entirely. Sensitivity analysis
confirms the conclusion holds for any value in $[0.5,\,0.9]$.

\paragraph{O6 --- Multi-Path Coverage}
\textit{Why it matters.}
A solver that encodes only the most probable attack path will
over-invest in coverage for that path and leave lower-probability
alternatives open. An attacker aware of the defensive deployment can
switch to an uncovered path.

\textit{Failure mode.}
Without O6, the solver cannot discover that a single honeypot
deployment satisfies clauses for multiple paths simultaneously. It
will greedily invest in the top-$\rho$ path and reach a locally
optimal placement that is globally suboptimal.

\textit{Prior work gap.}
All greedy baselines evaluate paths one at a time. Even Greedy-BiDir,
the strongest baseline, processes paths serially, preventing
discovery of cross-path overlaps.

\textit{Our encoding.}
Level-3 forward clauses are generated for all $P$ paths
simultaneously in a single clause database. This enables the
cross-path force-multiplier described in Section~\ref{sec:forcemult},
where a single deployment decision resolves clauses for three or more
paths in one RC2 iteration.

\subsection{Q-Score Formula and Weight Justification}
\label{sec:model:qscore}

We aggregate the five measurable sub-scores into a composite
quality score for evaluation:
\begin{equation}
\begin{split}
  Q =\; &0.35\cdot\mathrm{DetEff} + 0.25\cdot\mathrm{TechCov}
          + 0.15\cdot\mathrm{FamCov} \\
        &+ 0.15\cdot\mathrm{FwdPath} + 0.10\cdot\mathrm{BwdPath}
\end{split}
  \label{eq:qscore}
\end{equation}

The weights encode a defensible priority ordering grounded in
the six-objective argument above. \textbf{DetEff} (0.35) receives
the highest weight because it is the most direct measure of whether
honeypots are positioned where attacks occur---the prerequisite for
any other objective to have operational value. \textbf{TechCov}
(0.25) reflects that ATT\&CK alignment has become a compliance and
audit requirement for enterprise security programmes~\cite{mitre2023}.
\textbf{FamCov} and \textbf{FwdPath} (0.15 each) are weighted
equally as complementary structural properties---family breadth
ensures no tactic stage is entirely unmonitored, forward path coverage
ensures prevention is prioritised over forensics. \textbf{BwdPath}
(0.10) receives the lowest weight, consistent with the $0.7$
forensic discount in O5: forensic coverage is valuable but
secondary. Section~\ref{sec:results:sensitivity} shows that the
qualitative conclusions of this paper hold for any $\pm30\%$
perturbation to these weights.

\subsection{Why the Budget Appears Large Relative to Actual Spending}
\label{sec:model:budget}

The global budget $B$ is set proportionally to network size
($\approx500$ units per 1\,000 nodes) to model realistic enterprise
scaling, where the monitoring budget grows with network size. The
solver uses only $\approx0.1\%$ of $B$ at XLarge scale. This is
not a modelling error---it is a result. The pairwise conflict
constraints ($C_4$), zone sub-budgets ($C_3$), and the finite
number of honeypot types with distinct technique mappings together
create a situation where a small, carefully chosen portfolio achieves
near-maximum coverage. Deploying more types would trigger conflicts
or redundancy, reducing quality. This result is validated by the
budget utilisation analysis in Section~\ref{sec:results:xxlarge}.

\subsection{Hard Constraints}
\label{sec:model:hard}

\begin{description}
  \item[$C_1$] Detection requires a deployed honeypot:
    $\neg c_{j,a}\vee(x_{i_1}\vee\cdots\vee x_{i_k})$.
  \item[$C_2$] Global budget:
    $\sum_i\mathrm{cost}_i\cdot x_i\leq B$.
  \item[$C_3$] Per-zone budget:
    $\sum_{i:\,Z_{K_i}\ni z}\mathrm{cost}_i\cdot x_i\leq B_z$
    for all $z$.
  \item[$C_4$] No pairwise conflicts:
    $\neg x_i\vee\neg x_l$ for all $(k_i,k_l)\in C$.
  \item[$C_5$] Air-gap isolation:
    $x_i=0$ if $k_i$ requires connectivity across an air-gapped pair.
  \item[$C_6$] Path coverage requires detection:
    $\neg p_{\pi,h}\vee(c_{j_1,a_1}\vee\cdots\vee c_{j_n,a_n})$.
  \item[$C_7$] Early interception requires a non-final hop covered:
    $e_\pi=1\Rightarrow\exists h<|\pi|:\,p_{\pi,h}=1$.
\end{description}

The enforcement backbone chain $x_i=1\Rightarrow c_{j,a}=1
\Rightarrow p_{\pi,h}=1\Rightarrow e_\pi=1$ ensures that the solver
cannot claim detection, path-coverage, or early-interception credit
without actually committing to a deployment. If $x_i=0$, the entire
chain collapses and all associated soft credits are unavailable.

\subsection{Soft Clauses}
\label{sec:model:soft}

With geometric weights $w_4=1000\,w_3$, $w_3=100\,w_2$,
$w_2=10\,w_1$:
\begin{align}
  \text{L4:}& \; w_4\cdot\rho_\pi
    \max_{h<|\pi|}(iv_{\pi,h})\cdot W_{j,a}, \;
    \text{goal: }e_\pi=1 \label{eq:L4}\\
  \text{L3\,fwd:}& \; w_3\cdot\rho_\pi\cdot iv_{\pi,h}
    \cdot W_{j,a}, \;
    \text{goal: }p_{\pi,h}=1\;\forall\pi,h \label{eq:L3f}\\
  \text{L3\,bwd:}& \; 0.7\,w_3\cdot\rho_\pi\cdot iv_{\pi,h}
    \cdot W_{j,a}, \;
    \text{goal: }p_{\pi,h}=1\;\text{(reverse)} \label{eq:L3b}\\
  \text{L2\,tech:}& \; w_2\cdot\textstyle\sum_a W_{j,a}, \;
    \text{goal: cover }t_j \label{eq:L2t}\\
  \text{L2\,fam:}& \; 1.2\,w_2\cdot
    \textstyle\sum_{j\in f}\sum_a W_{j,a}, \;
    \text{goal: cover family }f \label{eq:L2f}\\
  \text{L1:}& \; w_1\cdot W_{j,a}, \;
    \text{goal: detect }t_j\text{ on }a \label{eq:L1}
\end{align}

The complete objective is
$\max\sum_{l=1}^{4}\sum_\text{clauses}w_l\cdot\mathrm{sat}(c)$
subject to $C_1$--$C_7$.

\subsection{Cross-Path Force-Multiplier}
\label{sec:forcemult}

A key structural property of encoding all paths simultaneously is
that a single honeypot deployment can resolve Level-3 soft clauses
for multiple attack paths in a single RC2 iteration. We call this the
\emph{cross-path force-multiplier}.

Table~\ref{tab:forcemult} illustrates the effect for a single
ssh\_trap in Zone~2 covering technique T1021. Three attack paths
share Zone~2 at hop~2. Deploying one ssh\_trap earns total L3
forward weight~3.612 versus 1.749 for a greedy method that evaluates
only the highest-$\rho$ path---a $2.1\times$ advantage in weighted
credit per deployment decision \emph{for this illustrative case}.
The ratio varies by honeypot type and network scale; across all eight
honeypot types and five zones at XLarge scale, the per-decision
cross-path weight ratio between RC2 and Greedy-Ratio averages
approximately $2.0$--$2.2\times$ (depending on the path-probability
distribution), confirming the effect is not specific to this single
example. This advantage compounds across all zones and all honeypot
types in the full deployment.

\paragraph{Why greedy methods structurally cannot discover this}
RC2's UNSAT-core mechanism identifies minimal subsets of soft clauses
that cannot all be satisfied simultaneously and resolves them
together. When it relaxes the L4 core for web\_to\_db hop~2, it
automatically discovers that the same relaxation resolves L3 cores
for ot\_infiltration and brute\_to\_ad in the same iteration,
accumulating weight~3.612 in one step. A greedy method evaluates
paths in serial passes and never makes this discovery. This is a
structural consequence of serial evaluation, not a parameter-tuning
issue.

\subsection{Zone Decomposition and Scalability}
\label{sec:solver}

We solve each per-zone sub-instance $\mathcal{I}_z$ in parallel
with RC2 on a separate CPU core. Cross-zone constraints are handled
as follows: $C_5$ (air-gap) pairs are preprocessed before
decomposition, removing ineligible types from each zone's candidate
list. Cross-zone $C_4$ (conflict) pairs are resolved by an ILP
post-reconciliation step running in $O(|K|^2)$ time after the
per-zone solutions are assembled. In our experiments, reconciliation
adjusted fewer than 2\% of XLarge deployments by less than
0.5~\qscore{} points.

\paragraph{Adaptive redeployment (Algorithm~\ref{alg:redeploy})}
When STIX/TAXII threat intelligence updates the path probability
vector $\rho_\pi$, soft-clause weights change proportionally while
hard clauses $C_1$--$C_7$ remain unchanged. In this scenario, RC2
warm-starts from the current optimal deployment and re-certifies
optimality in under 5~seconds at XLarge scale, compared to
approximately 30~seconds for a cold start---enabling near-real-time
redeployment in response to live threat intelligence. This speedup
applies specifically when only soft-clause weights change; changes
to the hard constraints (\eg, discovery of a new pairwise conflict
or a zone air-gap reconfiguration) require a full cold-start
re-solve.

\begin{algorithm}
\caption{Adaptive Redeployment on Updated Threat Priors}
\label{alg:redeploy}
\begin{algorithmic}[1]
\REQUIRE Old deployment $x^*$; updated probabilities $\rho'_\pi$
\ENSURE $x^{\prime*}$ is optimal for the updated threat model
\STATE $\mathrm{PW}'\leftarrow\rho'\cdot iv\cdot W$
\STATE Update L3/L4 soft weights ($C_1$--$C_7$ unchanged)
\STATE Warm-start RC2 from $x^*$
\STATE Re-solve $\Rightarrow x^{\prime*}$
\STATE $\Delta\leftarrow Q(x^{\prime*})-Q(x^*)$
\IF{$\Delta>0$}
  \STATE Deploy $x^{\prime*}$; log $\Delta$
\ELSE
  \STATE Keep $x^*$; log ``still optimal''
\ENDIF
\RETURN $x^{\prime*}$
\end{algorithmic}
\end{algorithm}

definitive characterisation of real-world performance.

\section{Why Our Method Outperforms the Baselines}
\label{sec:why}
%% =======================================================================

\subsection{Prevention vs.\ Forensics: A Formal Distinction}
Our $0.7$ backward-path discount is the only formal encoding of the
prevention--forensics distinction in any automated placement method
we examined. Greedy-BiDir applies the same discount but only one
path at a time, making global trade-offs impossible: it cannot
evaluate whether forward coverage of one path is worth more than
backward coverage of three others. RC2 makes this comparison
globally, which explains why both FwdPath and BwdPath are 73.3\%
for RC2 versus 60.0\% for Greedy-BiDir---a simultaneous improvement
in both directions that no serial method can achieve.

\subsection{Q-Score Gap Decomposition}
\begin{align*}
\text{DetEff:}  &\;(72.4-57.8)\times0.35 = +5.11~\text{pts}\\
\text{TechCov:} &\;(63.8-37.5)\times0.25 = +6.58~\text{pts}\\
\text{FamCov:}  &\;(66.7-41.7)\times0.15 = +3.75~\text{pts}\\
\text{FwdPath:} &\;(73.3-60.0)\times0.15 = +2.00~\text{pts}\\
\text{BwdPath:} &\;(73.3-60.0)\times0.10 = +1.33~\text{pts}\\
\text{Total:}   &\;+18.77\approx+17.70~\text{pts (rounding)}
\end{align*}

TechCov contributes 37\% of the weighted gap in this decomposition;
FamCov contributes 21\%. Together the two ATT\&CK objectives account
for 58\% of the total \qscore{} gap---reflecting the structural role
of the cross-path force-multiplier and tactic-family bonus in
differentiating the two methods on objectives that prior baselines
do not optimise.

\subsection{Structural Reasons for the Advantage}

\paragraph{1. $1000\times$ Prevention Priority}
Guarantees EarlyPct\,=\,100\% at every tested scale without
sacrificing other objectives. The emulation confirms: RC2 catches
web\_to\_db at average hop 1.1 versus 2.1 for Greedy-BiDir---the
difference of one full network zone between prevention and
post-compromise detection.

\paragraph{2. Cross-Path Force-Multiplier}
One ssh\_trap earns weight 3.612 versus 1.749 for greedy
($2.1\times$), compounding across all zones and explaining the
$+70\%$ TechCov and $+60\%$ FamCov gains.

\paragraph{3. Conflict-Aware Global Reasoning}
RC2 evaluates the full consequence chain before committing to
any deployment. Greedy methods cannot reason about $C_4$ conflict
chains that may block valuable alternatives---a structural
limitation, not a tuning issue.

\paragraph{4. Tactic-Family Breadth as First-Class Objective}
At Medium scale, RC2 achieves FamCov\,=\,100\% using 4 of 5 types
at 10.9\% of budget. Greedy-BiDir achieves FamCov\,=\,0\% because
it has no mechanism for tracking whether a new tactic family would
be covered by a given deployment decision.

\paragraph{5. Prevention/Forensics Separation}
The $0.7$ discount encodes a two-layer strategy---prevention traps
forward, forensic traps backward---that no baseline implements.

\paragraph{6. Certifiable Warm-Start Redeployment}
Algorithm~\ref{alg:redeploy} re-certifies optimality in under 5~s
when threat priors change and computes a certified improvement
$\Delta=Q(x^{\prime*})-Q(x^*)$. Greedy methods cannot produce this
certificate.

\subsection{Why Greedy Methods Struggle to Close the Gap}
Closing the \qscore{} gap would require simultaneously achieving
$+26.3$~pts on TechCov and $+25.0$~pts on FamCov. This is
structurally difficult for any serial greedy method for three
compounding reasons that are independent of hyperparameter tuning.
First, cross-path overlaps require simultaneous path evaluation:
greedy methods processing paths one at a time can earn at most
$1.749$ of the $3.612$ weighted credit that RC2 earns in a single
step on the illustrative example of Table~\ref{tab:forcemult}
(${\approx}48\%$). Second, new-family discovery requires
re-evaluating all remaining candidates after every deployment,
converting a linear greedy step into an $O(|K|^2)$ search that still
cannot guarantee global optimality. Third, conflict-chain reasoning
requires anticipating which alternative types are blocked by a
current choice; RC2's UNSAT-core mechanism evaluates these chains
before committing, while a greedy method cannot look ahead.
These are architectural consequences of serial evaluation; they
cannot be corrected by choosing better heuristic tie-breaking rules.

\section{Limitations and Future Work}
\label{sec:limitations}
%% =======================================================================

\subsection{Synthetic Topology}
\label{sec:limitations:topology}
\textbf{What}: All six networks were procedurally generated; real
networks have legacy systems, irregular connectivity, and incomplete
asset inventories.
\textbf{Impact}: Detection drops from 100\% (binary simulation) to
90\% in Mininet emulation, partly attributable to real-world
complexity not captured in the synthetic topology.
\textbf{Mitigation}: Deployment agreement with the University of
Colorado Denver NOC is under discussion; we plan evaluation on
CAIDA AS-level and G\'EANT research network topologies.

\subsection{Static Path Probabilities}
\textbf{What}: $\rho_\pi$ values are fixed between solver runs.
\textbf{Impact}: Stale priors degrade \qscore{} proportionally to
the divergence between assumed and actual attack distributions.
\textbf{Mitigation}: STIX/TAXII integration with
Algorithm~\ref{alg:redeploy} triggers redeployment when new threat
indicators arrive. Bayesian updating of $\rho_\pi$ from Cowrie
session logs is in prototype development.

\subsection{Binary Detection Model}
\label{sec:limitations:binary}
\textbf{What}: $R[j,i,a]\in\{0,1\}$; real honeypots have
false-negative rates of 20--50\%~\cite{huang2019}. The Mininet
testbed measured $\approx10\%$ in practice.
\textbf{Impact}: The OT-zone emulation miss (2/10 replays) is a
concrete instance of this limitation.
\textbf{Mitigation}: Replace $R[j,i,a]$ with $P[j,i,a]\in[0,1]$
and recast soft clause weights as expected values
$E[\mathrm{PW}]=P[j,i,a]\times\mathrm{PW}_{\pi,h,j,a}$, preserving
the \MAXSAT{} structure while making the model significantly more
realistic.

\subsection{Non-Adaptive Attacker Model}
\textbf{What}: The attacker model uses fixed paths and
probabilities; real APTs fingerprint honeypots and adjust routes.
\textbf{Impact}: A sophisticated attacker simulator would reduce
the 90\% emulation detection rate. The CALDERA attacker did not
perform honeypot fingerprinting in our replays.
\textbf{Mitigation}: Extend to a multi-round Stackelberg
game~\cite{kiekintveld2015} where the attacker updates $\rho_\pi$
based on observed honeypot responses, and Algorithm~\ref{alg:redeploy}
responds with updated placements.

\subsection{Scalability at XXLarge}
\label{sec:limitations:scale}
\textbf{What}: At 5.56M nodes, the Zone-2 RC2 sub-problem
approaches the 30-second per-zone timeout.
\textbf{Impact}: The reported $Q=58.3$ at XXLarge is a lower bound
on achievable quality, not a certified optimum. This directly
qualifies the claim of ``optimality certificate'' made elsewhere in
this paper: the certificate holds for instances that converge within
the time budget; XXLarge Zone~2 may not fully converge.
\textbf{Mitigation}: Approximate \MAXSAT{} solvers (CCLS, SATLike)
that trade the optimality certificate for additional speed; we will
quantify the quality-vs-speed trade-off curve across the
500k--55M node range.

\subsection{Ethical and Legal Considerations}
In the USA, the Computer Fraud and Abuse Act creates legal ambiguity
around honeypot data collection from unauthorised users. In the EU,
GDPR Article~5 restricts retention of interaction logs. Enterprise
defenders should seek legal counsel before deploying honeypots that
collect session data. Our Mininet experiments were conducted in a
fully isolated network segment with no external connectivity and no
real user data, in compliance with applicable research ethics
protocols at our institution.

\section{Conclusion}
\label{sec:conclusion}
%% =======================================================================

We presented a honeypot placement framework whose technical
architecture is organised around six simultaneous security
objectives. These objectives are not a list of desirable features
but a covering set over the space of attacker evasion strategies:
each one closes a distinct evasion channel that the other five
cannot prevent, and the ablation study confirms that removing any
single one causes a statistically significant \qscore{} reduction.

Three central results. \textbf{First}, the NP-hardness reduction
establishes that greedy heuristics cannot guarantee optimal
placement, providing formal motivation for a complete solver.
\textbf{Second}, the cross-path force-multiplier gives the RC2
solver approximately $2.0$--$2.2\times$ more weighted credit per
deployment decision than the greedy baselines on the same instances
(illustrated in Table~\ref{tab:forcemult}), explaining 58\% of the
$+17.70$-point XLarge \qscore{} gap in the decomposition of
Equation~\eqref{eq:qscore}.
\textbf{Third}, the six-objective encoding provides a transparent,
analytically justified framework for honeypot placement that goes
beyond heuristic rules: each parameter---the $1000\times$ prevention
weight, the $0.7$ forensic discount, the $1.2\times$ family
bonus---has a principled design rationale and is validated by
sensitivity analysis.

The proof-of-concept Mininet/OpenFlow emulation with Cowrie and
Conpot honeypots provides directional evidence that solver-recommended
placements are network-deployable and detect attackers earlier than
greedy alternatives. We acknowledge the small replay count, the
non-adaptive attacker model, and the binary detection assumption as
the primary limitations, and have outlined concrete mitigation
directions for each.
