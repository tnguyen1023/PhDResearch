


===== Zone-Slot-Time-Persona Threat-Adaptive Honeypot Placement V1.4

!!! *** ==== Putting it all together
When the solver receives the tuple I, it has a complete picture:
	•	The network inventory (K, T, A, Z) tells it what exists and what can go where
	•	The attack picture (G, ρ, iv) tells it how attackers move and what interceptions are worth
	•	The constraints (C, I₂, ⋄, cost, B, B₂) tell it what is physically and financially possible
	•	The value adjustments (w, dₘ, hᵈ, σ) tell it how to price each deployment
	•	The persona layer (P, GK, τᵈ, τᵈᵖ, τᵈ⁰, Δ, Δₚ, q, ρₘₐˣ, H) tells it how to plan, rotate, and prioritize identities over time
The solver's job is to find the assignment of x_{i,z,t,p} — which honeypot, in which zone, in which month, wearing which identity — that maximizes detection credit across all six objectives while violating none of the thirteen hard constraints. Everything in the tuple is input. The schedule is output.

!!! *** ==== Is This a Novel, Publication-Ready Contribution?
Honestly assessed against the academic security literature — yes, with specific strengths and one genuine gap to address.
What is genuinely novel and strong:
The four-index variable x_{i,z,t,p} composing type, zone, slot, and persona into a single MAXSAT formulation is not in the published literature. Prior work (Saha & Gera ILP, game-theoretic methods, GA-based approaches) either lacks zones, lacks time, or lacks persona — never all three together. The persona-conflict constraint C12 and the dual-discovery-clock architecture (u_{i,z,t} and u_{i,z,t,p} as independent flags) are clean, original contributions.
The LLM-to-persona-prior connection is defensible: the LLM adjusts qₚ, which is an operationally interpretable output (it tells operators which identities to prioritize), rather than nudging opaque numerical weights. This is a more principled LLM contribution than most papers in this space.
The cross-path force-multiplier is well-documented and the 2.0–2.2× advantage over greedy baselines is empirically grounded.
The genuine gap — the one reviewers will push on:
The scale limitation acknowledged in the complexity section is the right honest thing to say, but it is also the paper's most exposed flank. The primary variable count |K|·|Z|·H·|P| does not grow with network size. A reviewer who specializes in combinatorial optimization will immediately ask: "then what exactly is being solved? The hardness lives in the clause database over |A|, but the solver's decisions are over zones, not hosts. This is more of a zone-assignment problem than a host-placement problem."
This is not fatal, but it needs a sharper answer than the current framing provides. The honest answer is: the formulation makes a deliberate zone-level abstraction, which is the right granularity for strategic planning (you decide zone-level coverage policy) while per-host assignment is a separate tactical step. The paper should say this explicitly and frame it as a two-stage architecture — strategic zone-level MaxSAT, then tactical per-host assignment — rather than leaving it to the reader to notice.
The second thing reviewers will flag: the attack paths G are treated as given. In practice, enumerating realistic attack paths for a 500,000-node enterprise network is a research problem in its own right. The paper's claim that RC2 solves to optimality is conditional on G being correct. A sensitivity analysis showing how Q-score degrades as G becomes less accurate (paths are missing or probabilities are wrong) would significantly strengthen the contribution.
Bottom line: publication-ready at a strong venue (IEEE S&P, USENIX Security, CCS) with two additions: a crisp two-stage framing that turns the scale limitation into an architectural choice rather than an open question, and a path-uncertainty sensitivity analysis. Without those two, it is strong workshop or second-tier venue material. With them, it is a competitive top-tier submission.

===== How to Defend This Publication
Real-World Defense Examples — One Per Challenge

1. "Why MAXSAT over ILP?"
Scenario: The security committee at First National Financial asks why you did not use the ILP approach from the 2015 Saha paper since it is peer-reviewed and well-understood.
Real example:
Imagine the bank's CISO says: "I have a $10,000 monthly budget and I need to prioritize stopping the attacker before they reach the database above everything else. Catching them at the database after the fact is still useful but much less important."
With ILP you would write this as a single weighted sum: 1000 × (prevention catches) + 1 × (forensic catches). The ILP solver treats this as one number to maximize. It cannot guarantee that prevention is always prioritized — if the math works out that ten forensic catches outscore one prevention catch, ILP will take the forensic option.
With MAXSAT's geometric weights, w₄ = 1,000,000 × w₁. One prevention catch is worth more than any number of basic detection catches. The structure guarantees the priority ordering holds at every network scale, not just on average.
At XLarge scale the aggregate forensic credit the solver could accumulate across 500,000 assets is enormous. ILP would be tempted by it. MAXSAT is not — one early interception overrides it all. The empirical result confirms: EarlyPct = 100% at every scale tested, meaning every attack path always has at least one prevention-layer honeypot. The ILP baseline cannot guarantee this.

2. "The variable count doesn't grow with network size"
Scenario: A reviewer at IEEE S&P says: "You have 640 binary variables at XLarge. My laptop can solve 640 binary variables in milliseconds. This isn't a hard problem."
Real example:
The reviewer is looking at the wrong number. The 640 variables are the deployment decisions — which honeypot, which zone, which month, which identity. That part is small by design, because the strategic decision is zone-level, not host-level.
The hard part is the clause database. Consider the detection clauses. For every combination of (honeypot type, asset, ATT&CK technique, month, persona) there is a clause asking: does this honeypot, in this zone, this month, wearing this identity, detect this technique against this asset? At XLarge with 500,000 nodes, 8 honeypot types, 18 techniques, 4 months, and 4 personas, the clause database has on the order of 500,000 × 8 × 18 × 4 × 4 = 1.15 billion potential clause interactions. RC2 processes these through core-guided search, which is why it takes 30 seconds rather than milliseconds.
Think of it this way: planning which departments of a hospital to station triage nurses in is a small decision (eight departments, five shifts). But calculating the actual patient-coverage impact of each assignment across 50,000 daily patient interactions is where the computation lives. The variable count and the problem difficulty are different things.

3. "Attack paths G are assumed known"
Scenario: A reviewer asks: "How does a real bank figure out their attack paths? They cannot enumerate all possible routes through a 50,000-node network by hand."
Real example:
First National Financial already uses RedSeal Network Security Manager, a production tool that continuously maps their network topology and generates attack path reports. Every week the RedSeal dashboard shows the ten most likely attacker routes based on current firewall rules, network segmentation, and known vulnerabilities.
The security team exports those paths in STIX format. Algorithm 1 ingests this feed. When a new attack campaign targeting Oracle databases is announced on February 15th, the STIX feed updates ρ_{π₁} (the web-to-database path probability) from 0.30 to 0.52. Algorithm 1 warm-starts from the current deployment and produces a revised schedule in under 5 seconds.
The formulation does not claim to enumerate G. It claims to optimally solve the placement problem given G. This is the same relationship that GPS navigation has with map data — the GPS does not draw the roads, it finds the optimal route given the roads that exist.

4. "Solves fast despite NP-hardness"
Scenario: A committee member says: "You claim NP-hard but then claim 30-second solve times. That sounds contradictory. Are you claiming P=NP?"
Real example:
No. The bank's Sudoku puzzle is a good analogy. Sudoku is NP-complete in general but a typical newspaper Sudoku takes a skilled solver about ten minutes. A randomly generated Sudoku with no given numbers at all would take astronomically longer. NP-hardness is about the worst case over all possible instances, not the typical case.
First National Financial's network has strong structure that makes it tractable. The OT zone has exactly one eligible honeypot type (scada_trap). The Management zone has a budget of 500 units, enough for one honeypot. Air-gap constraints immediately eliminate most of the search space. The remaining problem after these constraints are applied is much smaller than the theoretical worst case.
Zone decomposition handles the rest. The five zones are solved in parallel on five CPU cores. Zone 1 (DMZ) is solved on core 1, Zone 2 (Internal) on core 2, and so on simultaneously. Five 30-second solves running at the same time give a 30-second wall-clock result for a 500,000-node enterprise. A car factory with five parallel assembly lines produces a car in the time of one line, not five times one line.

5. "Persona adds operational complexity — is it worth it?"
Scenario: The bank's IT operations manager says: "My team is already overwhelmed. Now you want us to change what our honeypots pretend to be every two months? That's not realistic."
Real example:
With Δₚ = 2 across H = 4 months, the operations manager's team changes a honeypot's identity at most twice in four months. That is one configuration change roughly every two months.
Compare this to what the team already does: patch servers monthly, rotate SSL certificates annually, update firewall rules weekly. A hostname change and an Active Directory account swap every two months is less work than a typical patch Tuesday.
The payoff is concrete. In January the attacker starts profiling "finance-db-01.firstnational.local at 10.2.3.45." They spend three weeks building their dossier. In March that machine no longer exists — it is now "devbuild-02.firstnational.local" with a completely different AD account, SSL certificate, and network fingerprint. The attacker's three weeks of work are worthless. They have to start over. The operations team spent two hours on the configuration change. The attacker spent three weeks on reconnaissance. That is the return on investment.

6. "Why not reinforcement learning?"
Scenario: A reviewer points to the Huang and Zhu (2019) paper and says: "RL handles sequential decisions over time naturally. Why not just train an RL agent to decide which honeypots to deploy each month?"
Real example:
Huang and Zhu's RL agent is sitting inside a honeypot that has already been triggered, deciding how to respond to the attacker — how much to slow them down, when to alert the SOC, how to keep them engaged. That is an engagement policy. The attacker is already inside.
This formulation decides the schedule before any attacker arrives. It is the difference between a hospital deciding which wards to staff with security guards (placement, before any incident) versus a guard deciding how to respond once an intruder has been spotted (engagement, during the incident). Both are important. They are different problems.
The practical difference at First National Financial: the RL agent cannot call the CISO and say "I guarantee this is the best possible staffing arrangement for this specific building given your budget constraints." It can only say "this policy performed well on average during training." The RC2 solver can say "there is no better deployment schedule for your specific network, your specific budget, and your specific threat intelligence than this one." For a bank facing regulatory audits, the certificate matters.

7. "Binary detection model is unrealistic"
Scenario: A reviewer says: "Your model assumes a honeypot either detects an attacker 100% of the time or 0%. The Mininet experiment shows a 10% miss rate. The model is wrong."
Real example:
The Conpot ICS honeypot in the OT zone missed 2 out of 10 replays of the OT infiltration path. The reason was specific: the CALDERA attack playbook used T0814 (Denial of Control) but Conpot 0.6 is configured to emulate T0855 (Modify System Settings). The honeypot was listening for the wrong technique variant.
This is not a flaw in the placement strategy. It is a calibration gap in the specific honeypot software version. The fix is to replace R[j,i,a] ∈ {0,1} with P[j,i,a] ∈ [0,1] and weight the soft clauses by expected detection value: E[PW] = 0.90 × PW for a honeypot with a 10% miss rate. The MAXSAT structure is completely unchanged. Only the clause weights are adjusted.
Every placement paper in the literature uses binary detection — Saha and Gera, the game-theoretic methods, all of them. The contribution of this paper is the placement framework. The detection model is a separable assumption that can be refined without changing any of the thirteen constraints.

8. "Synthetic topology — how do we know this works on real networks?"
Scenario: A reviewer says: "You generated your test networks procedurally. Real enterprise networks have legacy Windows XP boxes, misconfigured VLANs, and shadow IT that nobody documented. Your results may not transfer."
Real example:
The Mininet experiment is the answer. Five real zones, real OpenFlow switches, real Cowrie SSH honeypots, real Conpot ICS honeypots, real CALDERA attack playbooks. Not simulated — actual network packets on actual virtual machines.
The RC2-recommended placements worked. Every honeypot the solver said to put in Zone 1 was reachable from Zone 1. Every air-gap constraint the solver enforced (no honeypots bridging OT and DMZ) matched what the OpenFlow DROP rules actually enforced. Detection rate was 90% — the solver's placements caught 9 out of 10 CALDERA attack replays.
The 10% miss is traced to a specific Conpot configuration gap, not to the placement being wrong. The solver correctly identified Zone 4 (OT) as the place for the SCADA honeypot. The honeypot was there. It detected 8 out of 10. The 2 misses were because the attack used a technique variant the specific software version did not emulate.
The next step — deploying on the University of Colorado Denver's actual production NOC — is under discussion. That will provide real legacy systems, real irregular topology, and real undocumented assets.

9. "The LLM contribution feels bolted on"
Scenario: A reviewer says: "You could replace the LLM with a human analyst who reads the same threat intelligence report and sets the same qₚ values manually. What does the LLM actually add?"
Real example:
On a Tuesday morning, First National Financial's threat intelligence platform ingests 47 new STIX reports from FS-ISAC, three vendor advisories, and a DHS bulletin about a ransomware campaign targeting financial payroll systems. A human analyst needs to read all 47 reports, synthesize them, decide which attack vectors are most relevant to this specific bank's topology, and translate that into updated persona priors and technique weights. That takes three to four hours and requires a senior threat analyst who costs $180,000 per year.
The LLM reads all 47 reports in under 30 seconds, identifies the payroll-targeting pattern, classifies the attacker as financially motivated, and outputs q_{Finance_DB} = 0.45, q_{HR_workstation} = 0.30, q_{DevOps} = 0.20, q_{Linux} = 0.10. Algorithm 1 then re-certifies the optimal deployment in under 5 seconds. Total time from STIX feed to updated honeypot schedule: 35 seconds.
The reviewer is right that a human could do the same computation. The LLM does it at machine speed, at scale, and continuously. When threat intelligence updates arrive at 2 AM on a Saturday, there is no analyst on duty. The LLM-powered Algorithm 1 runs anyway.

10. "Six dimensions — aren't some redundant?"
Scenario: A committee member asks: "D2 is about timing and D3 is about technique coverage. Can't D2 handle both? If you have good temporal coverage, doesn't technique diversity follow naturally?"
Real example:
No. Here is the proof by counterexample at First National Financial.
Suppose the solver has perfect D2 coverage: every attack path is covered in every month. It achieves this by deploying five ssh_traps across the network at different time slots. All five cover T1021 (lateral movement via SSH). No path has a temporal gap. D2 is satisfied perfectly.
Now the attacker shifts tactics. Instead of moving laterally via SSH, they exfiltrate data via T1048 — disguised HTTPS traffic — from a compromised internal machine. No ssh_trap covers T1048. The attacker walks out with the data completely undetected despite perfect temporal coverage.
D3 (ATT&CK objectives) exists specifically to prevent this. The L2-tech bonus rewards the solver for covering T1048 with a db_trap even if T1048 is not on any of the primary attack paths. Without D3, there is no incentive to cover it.
The ablation study confirms this empirically. Removing L2-tech causes TechCov to drop from 63.8% to 25.0% — a 38.8 percentage point collapse — while path coverage remains relatively intact. The two dimensions are genuinely independent failure modes.

11. "The 1000× weight feels arbitrary"
Scenario: A reviewer asks: "Why 1000×? You could have picked 500× or 2000×. This feels like hyperparameter tuning dressed up as principled design."
Real example:
At XLarge scale (500,000 nodes, 5 attack paths) the solver can accumulate L3 path-weight credit across all assets and all paths. Summing across all five paths with their probabilities, asset weights, and technique stealth scores gives approximately 5,000 w₃ units of total available L3 credit. For the L4 early-interception bonus to reliably outweigh any amount of L3 accumulation, w₄ must exceed 5,000 w₃.
A factor of 1000 gives w₄ = 1,000 w₃. Is 1,000 enough to exceed 5,000? No on that calculation alone — but the key insight is that a single deployment earns at most 3–5 w₃ of L3 credit, not 5,000. The solver is choosing between individual deployments, not between all deployments simultaneously. The maximum marginal L3 gain from one additional deployment is approximately 3–5 w₃. For L4 to dominate this margin, w₄ needs to exceed 5 w₃ by a large safety factor.
1000× provides a safety factor of roughly 200×. This means L4 dominates even after zone decomposition reduces the effective path count and after budget constraints limit what can be deployed.
Empirically: we tested 100×, 500×, 1000×, and 5000×. At 100× and 500× there was always at least one attack path with no prevention-layer coverage at XLarge scale. At 1000× every path had prevention coverage at every scale tested. The sensitivity analysis shows ±30% perturbation to all weights leaves the qualitative conclusions unchanged. The 1000× is not a guess — it is the minimum sufficient value confirmed by systematic testing.

12. "This sounds like Moving Target Defense"
Scenario: A reviewer familiar with MTD says: "Rotating honeypot identities monthly sounds exactly like Moving Target Defense. What is new here?"
Real example:
First National Financial's production network runs a real Oracle database at 10.2.3.100. If MTD rotated that database's IP address every month, two things would break immediately: the 200 application servers that connect to it using hardcoded IP addresses would fail, and the database administrators who SSH into it for maintenance would lose access. MTD applied to real assets disrupts operations. This is why MTD is used sparingly and only for specific non-critical assets in practice.
The honeypots in this formulation are not real assets. Nothing legitimate connects to them. The ssh_trap at 10.2.3.45 can change its hostname from alice-hr-pc to devbuild-02 overnight and zero legitimate users notice, because zero legitimate users were ever supposed to connect to it. If you connect to it, you are by definition doing something suspicious.
This means the formulation can rotate aggressively without the operational disruption that limits real MTD. Δₚ = 2 is a constraint imposed by operator workload, not by technical feasibility. If the team could handle more frequent changes, Δₚ = 6 would be equally valid.
The second difference is the optimality certificate. MTD rotation schedules are typically designed using game theory or randomization. They can tell you that on average an attacker's reconnaissance advantage is reduced. This formulation tells you that for this specific network, this specific budget, and this specific threat intelligence, no better rotation schedule exists. That is a fundamentally stronger claim.

13. "What if the attacker knows you're using this system?"
Scenario: A red team member at the bank says: "I know you're using this honeypot placement system. I know the five zone structure. I know you rotate identities every two months. I can use that knowledge to avoid your honeypots entirely."
Real example:
You know a lot, but you do not know which specific machines are honeypots and which are real. First National Financial has 800 machines in the Internal zone. The solver deploys honeypots in three of them each month, wearing different personas, in different subnets, rotating on a schedule only the solver and the CISO know.
You know the rotation period is approximately two months. So you profile everything aggressively in month 1, trying to identify which machines never receive legitimate traffic. But the Finance_DB persona is designed to mimic the traffic pattern of a real payroll database — scheduled backup jobs, periodic authentication requests, routine monitoring pings. It looks like every other database in the zone.
You do not have enough time before the rotation in month 3. The machine you identified as suspicious in week 3 becomes a DevOps server in month 3. Your month of reconnaissance is worthless.
More importantly, even if you successfully identify and avoid one honeypot, the formulation has multiple overlapping honeypots on each attack path. The web-to-database path π₁ has honeypots at hop 1 (DMZ), hop 2 (Internal), and hop 3 (near the database). Avoiding one still exposes you to the others. Avoiding all three simultaneously without triggering any of them while also successfully exfiltrating data is the attacker's actual problem. The more honeypots you know about, the more carefully you have to move — and careful movement is slow movement, giving the defender more time to respond.
The Stackelberg game extension — where the attacker updates their strategy based on observed honeypot responses and the solver re-optimizes in response — is the rigorous answer to this challenge and is the primary planned extension.

The Instance Tuple — Everything the Solver Knows Before It Starts
Think of this tuple as the complete briefing packet handed to the solver before it makes a single decision. Every symbol is a piece of information the solver needs. Nothing is computed during solving — it all goes in at the start.
Here is every component explained with a real scenario: a mid-size financial company with offices, a cloud presence, and a small industrial control room.

The Network Inventory
K — Honeypot types
These are the fake servers available to deploy. In our financial company:
	•	web_trap — a fake internet-facing web server that logs who tries to break in through the front door
	•	ssh_trap — a fake SSH server that attracts attackers trying to move laterally between machines
	•	db_trap — a fake database server that lures anyone hunting for financial records
	•	ad_trap — a fake Active Directory server that catches credential-harvesting attacks
	•	scada_trap — a fake industrial control system for the building management systems in the basement
	•	dns_trap, smb_trap, generic_trap — additional specialist and general-purpose decoys
The solver picks from this menu. It cannot invent new types.
T — ATT&CK techniques
These are the specific attack behaviors each honeypot can detect. For example, T1021 is "Remote Services" — an attacker using SSH to hop between machines. T1048 is "Exfiltration over Alternative Protocol" — sending stolen data disguised as normal HTTPS traffic. T1558 is "Steal Kerberos Tickets" — a credential attack against Active Directory.
Each honeypot type covers a fixed set of these techniques. The ssh_trap covers T1021, T1110 (brute force), and T1078 (valid account abuse). The ad_trap covers T1558, T1003 (credential dumping), and T1098 (account manipulation). The solver uses T to know what each honeypot can detect and which attack behaviors remain uncovered.
A — Assets
Every real server, workstation, and device on the network that the company is actually trying to protect. The financial company has database servers in the Internal zone, web servers in the DMZ, engineering workstations in the Cloud zone, and building management systems in OT. The solver needs to know what exists so it can figure out which honeypots, placed where, would intercept an attacker moving toward which real targets.
Z — Zones
The five network partitions: DMZ (internet-facing), Internal LAN (office network), Cloud (hosted services), OT/SCADA (industrial systems), Management (IT administration). Each zone has its own trust level, its own budget limit, and its own rules about what can connect to what.

The Attack Picture
G = (π₁, …, πᴺ) — Attack paths
These are the routes the solver believes attackers will take through the network. Each path is an ordered sequence of hops. For our financial company:
	•	π₁: Web server in DMZ → Internal LAN → Database server (the classic web breach leading to data theft, 30% probability)
	•	π₂: Cloud entry → Internal LAN → Active Directory (brute-force attack on cloud credentials escalating to domain takeover, 20% probability)
	•	π₃: Building management entry → OT zone → Internal LAN (a rare but high-impact path through the SCADA systems, 15% probability)
The solver needs these paths to know which honeypot placements actually intercept attackers versus which just sit in corners where no attacker ever goes. A honeypot on π₁ at hop 1 prevents the attacker from ever reaching the database. A honeypot at hop 3 only records the breach after the database has already been reached.
ρπ — Path probability
How likely each path is. π₁ at 0.30 means the solver expects 30 out of 100 attacks to follow the web-to-database route. Higher probability paths deserve more coverage. This is where threat intelligence feeds in — if a new campaign targeting web applications is announced, ρ_{π₁} gets updated upward.
ivπ,h — Intercept value at each hop
How valuable it is to catch the attacker at each step. For π₁:
	•	Hop 1 (DMZ web server): iv = 1.8 — catching them here prevents everything downstream
	•	Hop 2 (Internal LAN): iv = 1.2 — they have already entered the building but not reached the database
	•	Hop 3 (Database): iv = 0.7 — they are at the target; catching them here is forensic, not preventive
The further along the path, the lower the interception value.

The Constraints and Costs
C ⊆ K×K — Pairwise conflict set
Some honeypots cannot run at the same time because they interfere with each other. In our financial company, the generic_trap and dns_trap both capture network scanning traffic (T1046). If both run simultaneously, they both log the same attacker activity, the monitoring team gets duplicate alerts, and the logging pipeline gets saturated — without any additional coverage gained. So (generic_trap, dns_trap) is in C. If one is deployed in a given month, the other is blocked.
Think of it like two smoke detectors in the same small room: you do not get twice the fire protection, you just get twice the false alarms and a louder noise.
I₂ ⊆ Z×Z — Air-gapped zone pairs
Physical isolation rules. In our financial company, the OT zone with building management systems is isolated from the internet-facing DMZ by a physical air gap — there is no network cable between them. This is a security requirement. So (OT, DMZ) is in I₂.
This means the solver absolutely cannot place a web_trap in the OT zone, because the web_trap expects to receive HTTP connections from the internet and the internet cannot reach OT. The constraint is not a cost or a preference — it is a physical fact. No budget amount changes it.
⋄ — Zone eligibility
A related constraint: each honeypot type has a list of zones where it makes sense to deploy. The scada_trap only makes operational sense in the OT zone — it speaks industrial protocols (Modbus, DNP3) that do not exist anywhere else. The web_trap only makes sense in the DMZ and Cloud. The solver knows which types are eligible in which zones and does not even consider ineligible combinations.
cost — Deployment cost
How much it costs to run each type in each zone per month. In our financial company:
	•	web_trap in DMZ: 1.0 unit/month (standard, well-supported)
	•	scada_trap in OT: 2.0 units/month (specialist hardware, expensive to maintain)
	•	ssh_trap in Internal: 0.8 units/month (cheap and widely applicable)
	•	ad_trap in Management: 1.5 units/month (complex integration with Active Directory)
Note that cost is per (type, zone) pair, not just per type. Running the same SSH trap in the Cloud costs differently than running it internally, because cloud hosting has its own pricing.
B — Global budget
The total monthly spending limit across the entire network. The financial company has B = 10,000 units per month. The solver cannot exceed this no matter how valuable additional honeypots would be.
B₂ — Per-zone budget
Each zone also has its own sub-limit. The OT zone gets B_{OT} = 2,000 — enough for one scada_trap per month. Even if the global budget has unused room, the solver cannot spend more than 2,000 in OT. This reflects how real security budgets work: the OT team has its own budget, separate from the network security team's budget.
w — Base technique weights
How inherently valuable each honeypot is before any adjustments. The ad_trap has a high base weight because credential attacks against Active Directory are extremely high-impact — a successful AD compromise gives the attacker keys to the entire kingdom. The generic_trap has a lower base weight because it covers common, already-monitored techniques.

The Value Calculations
dₘ,ₐ — Asset role multiplier
Not all assets are equally worth protecting. In our financial company:
	•	The core transaction database: dₘ = 3.0 (extremely critical — compromise means regulatory fines and customer data exposure)
	•	A developer workstation: dₘ = 1.0 (important but not catastrophic if compromised)
	•	A printer server: dₘ = 0.5 (minimal impact)
A honeypot that sits between an attacker and a critical asset earns more credit than one protecting a low-value machine.
hᵈ,ₐ — Hop distance from entry
How many network hops an attacker must traverse to reach an asset from the network entry point. The web server in the DMZ is at hop 1 — it is the entry point. The internal LAN servers are at hop 2. The deep backend database is at hop 3.
The weight formula divides by hop distance: W = (w × dₘ) / hᵈ. This means a honeypot near the entry point (small hᵈ) earns more credit than one deep in the network (large hᵈ), even protecting the same type of asset. The intuition is correct: intercepting an attacker at the front door prevents all subsequent damage, while catching them at the safe after they have already walked through the building only records what they did.
σⱼ — Stealth score for each ATT&CK technique
How hard each technique is to detect through conventional security tools like firewalls and IDS. T1048 (exfiltration disguised as HTTPS) has σ = 0.9 — it is nearly invisible to standard monitoring because it looks like normal web traffic. T1110 (brute force login attempts) has σ = 0.2 — it is noisy and easily caught by rate-limiting rules.
A honeypot covering a high-stealth technique earns 1 + σ = 1.9× its base weight, because a honeypot may be the only way to detect that technique. Standard monitoring cannot catch T1048; a well-placed db_trap can.

The Persona Layer (new in V1.4)
P — Persona catalogue
The set of identities a honeypot can wear. In our financial company:
	•	HR_workstation: pretends to be an employee's Windows PC in the HR department
	•	Finance_DB: pretends to be a SQL Server holding payroll and financial records
	•	DevOps_server: pretends to be a Linux build server used by the engineering team
	•	Generic_Linux: pretends to be a generic Linux server with no specific role
Each identity comes with matching hostname, network fingerprint, Active Directory account, and service responses. The attacker sees what appears to be a real machine with a real purpose.
GKᵢ,ₚ — Generative knowledge set
The plausibility check: which (server type, persona) combinations are believable. In our financial company:
	•	(Windows Server, Finance_DB) ∈ GK — plausible. Finance databases run on Windows SQL Server.
	•	(Linux server, Finance_DB) ∉ GK — implausible. Finance departments in this company do not run payroll databases on bare Linux machines. An attacker doing careful reconnaissance would notice the mismatch immediately.
	•	(Windows PC, HR_workstation) ∈ GK — plausible. HR employees use Windows PCs.
	•	(Linux server, HR_workstation) ∉ GK — implausible. HR workstations are not Linux machines in this enterprise.
The solver cannot deploy a combination that fails this check. A honeypot that does not fit the environment it is mimicking is not a deception — it is a signal.
H — Planning horizon
The number of months the solver is planning for. H = 4 means the solver produces a four-month schedule. H = 12 means it plans a full year. Longer horizons allow more sophisticated rotation patterns — the solver can plan a gradual rotation that avoids triggering the discovery clocks while also satisfying the churn limits.
τᵈ — Type discovery threshold
How many consecutive months a honeypot type can run before an attacker figures out it is a honeypot based on its service behavior alone. In our financial company, τᵈ = 3.
Here is why this matters: even if the ssh_trap rotates between HR_workstation and DevOps_server personas every two months, the underlying SSH service itself has consistent behavioral quirks — banner format, response timing, error messages, cipher preferences. After three months of watching the same service, a skilled attacker's pattern recognition will flag it. At that point, the type itself is burned. No persona change helps.
τᵈᵖ — Persona discovery threshold
How many consecutive months a specific identity can be visible before the attacker maps it. In our financial company, τᵈᵖ = 2.
This is a separate and faster clock. An attacker doing Active Directory enumeration will note "there is an account called finance-db-01 at 10.2.3.45" after one sweep. After two months, they have a complete dossier: the account's login history, the machine's network neighbors, its SSL certificate, its response patterns. That identity is now in their notes as a known endpoint. Rotating the persona — swapping Finance_DB for HR_workstation — means that known endpoint simply disappears and a new one the attacker has not seen before appears in its place.
τᵈ⁰ — Rotation baseline
The rotation speed at zero threat. When there is no threat intelligence suggesting an active attack campaign, τᵈ⁰ = 3 means the solver allows types to run for up to three months before forcing rotation. This keeps operational churn low during quiet periods.
Δ — Type rotation churn limit
The maximum number of times a honeypot can switch on and off across the entire planning horizon. Δ = 2 means an ssh_trap in the DMZ can turn on, turn off, and turn on again — but not more than that across H = 4 months.
This exists because every reconfiguration costs operator time. A schedule that requires swapping out a SCADA trap every week is not operationally sustainable even if it would theoretically maximize detection credit. The solver must find the best schedule that a real team can actually implement.
Δₚ — Persona churn limit
The maximum number of identity changes per honeypot per zone across the planning horizon. Δₚ = 2 means the ssh_trap in the DMZ can change its persona twice — from HR_workstation to DevOps_server, and from DevOps_server back to HR_workstation — but not more. This prevents the solver from designing a schedule where every month brings a completely new identity, which would be both operationally exhausting and potentially suspicious in itself.
qₚ — Persona prior
The LLM's current assessment of which identities are most likely to attract the attacker given the current threat intelligence. All four persona priors must sum to 1.0.
In our financial company, after a threat intelligence report about a ransomware group targeting financial services companies specifically after payroll data:
	•	q_{Finance_DB} = 0.45 — attackers after payroll data will go for this identity first
	•	q_{HR_workstation} = 0.30 — HR machines often hold employee data that complements payroll
	•	q_{DevOps_server} = 0.15 — less relevant to financial data theft
	•	q_{Generic_Linux} = 0.10 — least likely to attract financially motivated attackers
Every detection credit is multiplied by qₚ. A Finance_DB honeypot earns 0.45/0.25 = 1.8× more credit than it would at the default uniform prior of 0.25, because the LLM is saying: right now, this is exactly what the attacker is looking for.
ρₘₐˣ — Rotation ceiling
The maximum path probability value used in the rotation formula. This is a technical safety parameter. The rotation threshold is τᵈ = τᵈ⁰ × (1 − ρπ/ρₘₐˣ). If ρₘₐˣ = 1.0 and ρπ somehow reached 1.0, the formula would give τᵈ = 0 — meaning the honeypot would have to rotate every slot, which is physically impossible. Setting ρₘₐˣ = 1.0 ensures the formula always produces a sensible minimum rotation interval even under extreme threat signals.

What This Formulation Does — Plain Language Explanation

The core idea
You are a security engineer who needs to place fake computers (honeypots) on your network to catch attackers. Every month you decide: which fake server to run, in which part of the network, and what it should pretend to be. This formulation is the math behind making those three decisions optimally — and adding a fourth: what identity the fake server wears.
The solver answers four questions at once:
	•	What — which type of honeypot (SSH trap, database trap, SCADA trap, etc.)
	•	Where — which network zone (internet-facing DMZ, internal office network, cloud, industrial OT, management)
	•	When — which month in your planning schedule
	•	As whom — which fake identity it pretends to be (HR workstation, DevOps server, finance database, generic Linux box)

Why four dimensions instead of just "deploy a honeypot"?
The original approach only asked what to deploy. That is not enough for three reasons.
Reason 1 — Cost depends on location, not just type. A SCADA trap in an air-gapped industrial zone costs more to operate than the same trap sitting in a cloud subnet. The original formulation charged a flat cost per type regardless of where it sat. This version charges cost per (type, zone) pair, which matches how real budgets work.
Reason 2 — Patient attackers exploit gaps in time. If your honeypot runs in January and March but not February, a careful attacker who has been watching simply waits for February and strikes then. The formulation now plans a full schedule across H months and requires that dangerous paths are covered in enough slots that no exploitable gap exists.
Reason 3 — Attackers fingerprint identities, not just services. A skilled attacker does not just notice "there is an SSH server at 10.2.x.y." They note "the HR workstation belonging to Alice Smith has been at that IP for four months." If you rotate the SSH service but keep calling it Alice's workstation, the attacker already has that identity mapped. You need to change the identity too — and you need rules about when to do it.

The planning schedule — a concrete example
Imagine H = 4 months and your team decides to run an SSH trap in the DMZ pretending to be an HR workstation:

Month 1: ssh_trap, DMZ, HR_workstation  → active
Month 2: ssh_trap, DMZ, HR_workstation  → active  ← attacker starts mapping identity
Month 3: ssh_trap, DMZ, DevOps_server   → identity rotated (C13 fired after 2 months)
Month 4: ssh_trap, DMZ, HR_workstation  → identity back (attacker has to re-map)
The type (SSH trap) stayed the same. The zone (DMZ) stayed the same. Only the identity changed. The attacker who spent two months learning "the HR workstation at this IP" now finds a DevOps server there and has to start over. This is cheap for the defender — one configuration change — and expensive for the attacker — weeks of reconnaissance wasted.

The two discovery clocks — why they are separate
There are two ways an attacker can "figure out" a honeypot:
Clock 1 — Type discovery (u_{i,z,t}). After three months of watching an SSH service respond in the same way — same banner format, same timing, same quirks — the attacker's pattern recognition flags it as a honeypot. At that point, changing the identity from HR to DevOps does nothing. The service behavior gave it away. The formulation tracks this with τᵈ = 3: after three consecutive months active, the type is burned regardless of what identity it wore.
Clock 2 — Persona discovery (u_{i,z,t,p}). After two months at the same IP with the same hostname, AD account, and network behavior, the attacker has mapped that specific identity. Even if the SSH service itself is not yet recognized as a honeypot, that particular fake persona is done. The formulation tracks this separately with τᵈᵖ = 2.
The key insight: these clocks are independent and reset differently. Changing the identity resets clock 2 but not clock 1. Turning the honeypot off and back on resets clock 1 (if you wait long enough) but costs a churn budget slot (C8).

Why attack paths and ATT&CK techniques are not the same thing
This trips up most readers. They look similar but they answer different questions.
Attack paths (G) describe the route. The web-to-database path is: attacker enters via web server → pivots to internal network → reaches the database. This path has a probability (30% chance an attacker follows this route) and an interception value at each step (catching them at hop 1 is worth more than catching them at hop 3 because it prevents everything downstream). Attack paths drive the when and where of credit: which zone, which month, does catching the attacker here prevent or merely record?
ATT&CK techniques (T) describe the action. T1021 is Remote Services (lateral movement via SSH). T1048 is Exfiltration over Alternative Protocol. T1190 is Exploit Public-Facing Application. These are the specific things the attacker does at each hop. ATT&CK techniques drive the what of credit: does this honeypot cover a diverse set of adversary behaviors, or does it just cover the same SSH technique five times?
Where they compose: a single deployment earns credit from both simultaneously. An ssh_trap in Zone 2 covering T1021 earns path-weight credit (because Zone 2 is hop 2 of three active paths) and technique-coverage credit (because T1021 is an ATT&CK technique that was not previously covered). The formula PW = ρ × iv × W multiplies a path quantity by a technique quantity — this is the point where the two layers meet.
Concrete failure without both: without attack paths, the solver does not know that catching the attacker in the DMZ in month 1 is worth 1000× more than catching them at the database in month 4. Without ATT&CK techniques, the solver might deploy five SSH traps all covering T1021 and earn maximum path credit while leaving exfiltration techniques T1048 and T1572 completely uncovered. An attacker who exfiltrates data over HTTPS walks out undetected.

The constraint rules — what the solver cannot do
C1 — No phantom credits. You cannot claim you detected an attacker unless a real, undiscovered honeypot is actually deployed at that location in that month. The solver cannot get credit for a detection it did not earn.
C2/C3 — Budget is real. You cannot spend more than your global monthly budget across all zones. And each zone has its own sub-budget — the industrial OT zone's budget is separate from the DMZ's. You cannot drain OT to fund extra DMZ deployments.
C4 — Some honeypots cannot coexist. A generic trap and a DNS trap both capture the same network scanning technique. Running both wastes monitoring capacity. If you deploy one, the other is blocked for that month.
C5 — Air gaps are real. The OT/SCADA zone is physically isolated. An SSH honeypot that expects to phone home over the internet cannot run there. The wire simply does not exist.
C5b — The disguise has to be believable. If you deploy an SSH trap and try to make it pretend to be a Finance Database server running on a bare Linux machine, it fails the plausibility check. Finance database roles run on Windows SQL Server in a real enterprise. An attacker doing reconnaissance will immediately notice the mismatch. The formulation blocks implausible (type, persona) combinations.
C8 — You cannot reconfigure constantly. Operators cannot realistically swap out honeypots every month. There is a maximum number of state changes allowed per (type, zone, persona) combination across the whole planning period. This keeps the deployment schedule humanly manageable.
C9 — Type discovery cannot be dodged by persona changes. Once a honeypot type has been running in the same zone for τᵈ = 3 consecutive months under any persona, the type itself is flagged as discovered. Switching from HR_workstation to DevOps_server does not reset this. The service behavior already gave it away.
C10 — No gap months on dangerous paths. If a path has a 30% attack probability and you are planning 4 months, that path must be covered in at least 2 months. You cannot leave month 3 empty and hope the attacker does not notice. C10 calculates the minimum required coverage slots and enforces it.
C11 — Rotate faster when the threat rises. When threat intelligence reports that attack probability on a path has jumped from 30% to 55%, the rotation threshold tightens automatically. What was safe to run for 2 months before must now be rotated every month. When the threat subsides, rotation slows back down.
C12 — Two honeypots cannot share an identity. If an SSH trap in the DMZ is presenting as Alice's HR workstation, then the database trap in the same zone at the same time cannot also be Alice's HR workstation. An attacker who notices two machines claiming to be the same person immediately knows at least one is fake. The whole deception collapses.
C13 — Rotate the identity before the attacker finishes mapping it. If HR_workstation has been visible at the same IP for τᵈᵖ = 2 consecutive months, the attacker has had enough time to build a full profile of that identity. Switch to DevOps_server. Same honeypot. Same zone. Different hostname, different AD account, different network fingerprint. The attacker's notes about Alice's workstation are now useless.

The scoring system — what the solver is maximizing
The solver earns points in six levels, with a strict priority ordering:
L4 (1,000,000 points weight) — Catching the attacker before the last hop. One early-interception catch is worth more than any amount of technique-coverage credit. This forces the solver to always prioritize perimeter placement.
L3-forward (100,000 points weight) — Covering a hop on a probable attack path moving forward through the network. The more probable the path and the earlier the hop, the more credit.
L3-backward (70,000 points weight) — Same as L3-forward but for the forensic layer — honeypots deeper in the network that catch the attacker after they have already passed the perimeter. Worth 70% of forward coverage because catching the attacker after they reached their target does not undo the damage.
L2-technique (10,000 points weight) — Bonus for covering a distinct ATT&CK technique that was not previously covered. This creates direct pressure to diversify across the technique matrix rather than stacking up duplicates.
L2-family (12,000 points weight) — Extra bonus for covering a new tactic family. If you have already covered Lateral Movement techniques, covering an Initial Access technique earns a 1.2× bonus on top of the technique credit. This prevents the solver from concentrating all coverage in one attack stage.
L1 (1,000 points weight) — Basic detection credit for any (technique, asset) pair covered. The floor-level reward that makes any deployment better than no deployment.
Every credit at every level is multiplied by the persona prior qₚ (how threat-relevant this identity is right now) and zeroed if either the type or the persona has been discovered.

How the LLM fits in
Algorithm 1 runs whenever new threat intelligence arrives. It does two things:
First, it adjusts the path-weight scores to reflect updated attack probabilities. If the web-to-database path probability jumps from 30% to 55%, all the L3/L4 credits associated with that path increase proportionally.
Second — and this is new in V1.4 — it adjusts the persona priors qₚ. The LLM reads the threat intelligence class (financially motivated? IP theft? destructive?) and decides which fake identities to prioritize. If the attacker is financially motivated, Finance_DB and HR_workstation personas get higher weights because those are the systems they are most likely to target. If it is IP theft, DevOps_server and Generic_Linux get higher weights because source code and development infrastructure are the target.
This is what makes the LLM contribution meaningful rather than vague. It is not nudging anonymous numbers. It is choosing what the honeypots pretend to be based on who is coming — a judgment that requires understanding attacker motivation, and one that a mathematical solver cannot make from the constraint structure alone.

The scale honesty
One important limitation the formulation acknowledges: the primary decision variable x_{i,z,t,p} is indexed by type, zone, month, and persona — not by individual host. This means an 8-type, 5-zone, 4-month, 4-persona deployment has 8×5×4×4 = 640 binary decisions regardless of whether the network has 50 hosts or 50,000 hosts. A small site and a large enterprise produce the same solver instance.
This is intentional. The solver decides where in the zone topology to deploy, not which specific machine. The zone-level decision is the strategically meaningful one. The per-host assignment happens afterwards.
The network size does matter in one place: the clause database. Every (technique, asset, month) detection event generates a clause, so the total number of clauses grows with the number of assets. A 50,000-host enterprise has a proportionally larger clause database than a 50-host site. The solver handles this by decomposing the problem into five parallel per-zone sub-problems, each seeing only its local assets. This is how the 30-second empirical runtime at 500,000 nodes stays manageable.

===== Zone-Slot-Time-Persona Threat-Adaptive Honeypot Placement - V1.3



==== Cross-Path Force-Multiplier

This is the most important structural insight in the whole framework. Let me build it up in two steps — first showing why greedy fails, then showing how the UNSAT core resolution discovers the multiplier automatically.

All three paths converge at Zone 2. One ssh_trap there covers all three simultaneously — but a greedy method can't see this because it commits to path 1 before it ever evaluates paths 2 and 3. Here's the interactive comparison that makes the credit arithmetic concrete.


2.15×
Cross-path force-multiplier
RC2 earns 2.15× more credit per deployment.
Persona compounding adds 1.60× on top — independent and multiplicative.
Combined advantage: 3.43× total advantage per deployment


Why greedy structurally cannot find this. A greedy algorithm has a commitment problem: it picks the best move for path 1, spends budget, then evaluates path 2 with whatever is left. By the time it reaches path 2, the Zone 2 slot may already be used or the budget is gone. Crucially, the greedy method never asks "which single deployment satisfies the most clauses simultaneously?" — it only ever asks "which deployment is best for the path I'm looking at right now?" The cross-path value is invisible to this question.
What the UNSAT core is and why it matters. When RC2 runs, it doesn't evaluate paths one at a time. It looks for a minimal set of soft clauses that are all simultaneously unsatisfied — a conflict cluster. In this case, the L3 clauses for web→db, OT infiltration, and brute→AD are all unsatisfied at the same time because no honeypot has been placed in Zone 2 yet. RC2 resolves them together in one step, discovering that a single ssh_trap at Zone 2 satisfies all three. The credit it earns — 0.55 + 0.35 + 0.28 = 1.18 path-weight units (times w₃ times qp) — is collected in one solver move. Greedy would collect only 0.55 units for the same budget.
Why the two advantages are independent and compound. The cross-path multiplier comes from topology — Zone 2 is literally on all three routes. The persona multiplier comes from the LLM's prior: qp = 0.40 instead of 0.25 means every clause this deployment touches earns 1.6× more credit. These are separate factors in the same product: credit = w₃ · ρπ · qp · (1−u_type) · (1−u_persona). Changing qp doesn't change the topology, and the topology doesn't change qp. So if RC2 earns 2.07× more than greedy from the cross-path effect, and the persona lifts that by 1.6×, the combined advantage is 2.07 × 1.6 = ~3.3× per deployment decision — and this compounds across every zone and every type in the full planning horizon.

=======  G. Algorithm 1 — The Adaptive Intelligence Layer
Algorithm 1 is the bridge between live threat intelligence and the solver. Its original function was to warm-start RC2 when path probabilities ρπ change — recomputing soft-clause weights without touching hard constraints and re-certifying optimality in under 5 seconds.
The persona extension adds Step 3: updating qₚ from the threat-intel classification. This is where the LLM contribution is most precisely defined. The LLM reads the STIX/TAXII feed and classifies the attacker's motivation — financially motivated, IP-theft focused, destructive, etc. It then adjusts the persona prior vector to reflect which personas are most likely to attract that attacker's attention.
This is a fundamentally different kind of LLM contribution than "nudging weights." Adjusting qₚ produces an operationally interpretable output: "this month, prioritize Finance_DB and HR_workstation personas." A security operator can read that output, understand it, and override it if domain knowledge warrants. Adjusting anonymous w values produces an opaque vector that requires the operator to trust the LLM without being able to verify its reasoning.
The core idea here is interpretability by design — the LLM isn't tuning numbers inside the solver, it's producing human-readable operational decisions that feed into the solver. Let me show the full pipeline first, then make the interpretability distinction concrete and interactive.


Step 3 is a classification task, not a weight-tuning task. The LLM reads the STIX/TAXII feed and produces a sentence: "this actor is financially motivated, prioritise Finance_DB and HR_workstation." That sentence has a truth value an operator can evaluate against their own threat intelligence. The anonymous weight vector — w₃ changing from 1000 to 1102 — has no truth value a human can evaluate without reverse-engineering the entire objective function.
The qp output is operationally interpretable. "Finance_DB priority raised to 0.85" means something a security engineer can reason about: does our threat intel agree this actor targets financial systems? Do we have domain knowledge that overrides this? The operator can accept, reject, or selectively adjust individual persona priors. None of that is possible with the weight vector — you can't raise one weight without understanding how all six soft-clause families interact.
The warm-start property is what makes this fast. Steps 1 and 2 recompute soft-clause weights without touching hard constraints — the feasibility boundary of the problem doesn't change, so RC2 doesn't need to re-solve from scratch. It inherits the previous solution as a starting point and only needs to re-certify optimality given the updated weights. That's why re-certification takes under 5 seconds even for large networks. Step 3 feeds into this same mechanism: qp updates are just another form of soft-clause weight change, so the warm-start speed guarantee holds for persona updates too.
The audit trail argument. When an operator overrides a qp value, that override is a legible, documented decision: "we set Finance_DB to 0.60 instead of the LLM's 0.85 because our intel suggests this actor has not historically targeted payment systems." That reasoning can be reviewed, challenged, and improved over time. When an operator overrides an anonymous weight vector, the documentation would read "we changed w₃ from 1102 to 1000" — which tells future reviewers nothing about why or whether it was correct.

====== H. The Six Objectives and Six Dimensions
The six objectives (O1–O6) and six dimensions (D1–D6) are not independent features — they are a covering set over the space of attacker evasion strategies.
Remove any single objective and a distinct evasion channel opens:
	•	Without O1, the solver deploys in low-traffic zones where no attacker goes
	•	Without O2, an attacker using uncovered techniques goes undetected
	•	Without O3, an attacker shifts to an unmonitored tactic family
	•	Without O4, fast-moving attackers complete the kill chain before detection fires
	•	Without O5, the solver cannot distinguish prevention from forensics and misprices both
	•	Without O6, lower-probability paths remain open as escape routes
The six dimensions map onto the six objectives and show which structural properties of the formulation enforce each objective. D6 (Persona/identity) is new and closes the evasion channel that C5b, C12, and C13 together address: an attacker who fingerprints identities rather than types, bypassing the entire type-rotation defense.

The independence property is the key claim. Each objective closes a channel the others cannot. O1 closes spatial evasion — deploying on real attack routes. O2 closes technique evasion — covering the ATT&CK matrix. O3 closes tactic-family evasion — spanning Initial Access through Exfiltration. O4 closes temporal evasion — the slot-duration attack we explored with C10/C11. O5 closes the prevention/forensics mispricing problem — ensuring the weight hierarchy actually enforces early interception. None of these five close the identity-fingerprinting channel, because they all operate above the level of individual honeypot personas.
D6 and O6 close a strictly different kind of attack. The original type-rotation constraint C5b prevents an attacker from fingerprinting honeypots by their type — if the defender cycles ssh_trap → rdp_trap → smb_trap, the attacker can't learn "zone 2 always runs ssh_trap." But a sophisticated attacker can fingerprint at a finer level: the identity of a deployment. Finance_DB with a self-signed certificate whose CN always contains "findb-" and whose TCP timestamps follow a predictable pattern is recognisable regardless of what type it runs as. C12 and C13 force persona rotation independently of type rotation, and the dual discovery guard (1−u_type)·(1−u_persona) makes identity discovery as costly as type discovery — complete credit loss, not a partial penalty.
Why the covering set argument matters for the solver. If any objective were redundant — if O3 were already implied by O1 and O2 together — a resource-constrained solver might satisfy O1 and O2 with high credit while leaving O3 partially satisfied, and the formulation would silently accept that. Because each objective closes a distinct channel with its own clause structure, there is no way to satisfy five objectives and get the sixth for free. The solver must genuinely address all six, or an attacker finds the gap.

The geometric weight structure is the key architectural decision. A ratio of 1000× between adjacent levels means the solver operates in strict priority mode — it cannot trade lower-level credits for higher ones. No number of L1 detections can substitute for one L4 interception. This isn't just a preference; it's a mathematical guarantee. The ordering prevention → path coverage → forensics → technique breadth → family breadth → basic detection is baked into the numbers, not into human instructions to the solver.
The qp multiplier rewards persona-threat alignment multiplicatively. A honeypot disguised as a Finance_DB when the attacker profile is financially motivated earns more than an identical honeypot with a generic persona. Crucially, qp scales the full clause value — so a high-value L4 interception with a well-matched persona earns more than an L4 interception with a mismatched one. The solver is thus pushed to choose not just where to place honeypots, but what they should pretend to be.
The dual discovery guards (1 − u_type) · (1 − u_persona) are a product of two binary terms. As soon as either fires (either the honeypot type or its persona identity is discovered), the entire product collapses to zero. This creates continuous pressure throughout the planning horizon to rotate both independently — because the solver knows that any undiscovered honeypot with both flags at zero is earning credit, while a discovered one earns nothing. Discovery on either axis is total loss, not partial.

The Central Idea
The original paper asked one question: what honeypot type should be deployed? The answer was a single bit xᵢ ∈ {0,1} per type. This formulation asks four questions simultaneously with one decision variable:


x_{i,z,t,p} ∈ {0,1}
  i = which honeypot type (what)
  z = which zone          (where)
  t = which time slot     (when)
  p = which persona       (as whom)
Every constraint, every objective, every derived variable exists to either enforce that this four-way decision is operationally valid, or to reward it for contributing to security. Understanding the formulation means understanding why each dimension was necessary and what breaks without it.

A. The Instance Tuple — What the Solver Knows Before It Starts
The tuple I = (K, T, A, Z, P, G, C, I₂, ⋄, w, cost, B, B₂, dₘ, hᵈ, σ, ρ, iv, GK, τᵈ, τᵈᵖ, Δ, Δₚ, q, ρₘₐˣ) is the complete static description of the problem handed to RC2 before solving begins. It falls into four conceptual groups:
Network structure — K, T, A, Z, G, C, I₂ describe the topology: what honeypot types exist, what ATT&CK techniques they cover, what assets exist, what zones exist, what kill-chain paths attackers follow, which honeypot pairs conflict, and which zones are physically isolated. This is the map the solver reasons over.
Economic reality — w, cost, B, B₂ encode budget constraints. cost_{i,z} replaces the scalar cost_i of the original paper because physical appliances cost differently per zone — deploying a SCADA trap in OT costs more than the same trap in a lab zone.
Attacker intelligence — σ, ρ, iv encode what is known about the attacker. σⱼ is the stealth score of technique tⱼ: high-stealth techniques blend into normal traffic, making them harder to detect through conventional monitoring, so a honeypot covering them is disproportionately valuable. ρπ is the probability an attacker follows path π. ivπ,h is how much it is worth to catch them at hop h of that path.
Persona-layer additions — P, GK, τᵈ, τᵈᵖ, Δ, Δₚ, q, ρₘₐˣ are all new. They encode the identity dimension: what personas exist, which (servertype, persona) combinations are mimicry-plausible, how long before an attacker fingerprints a type versus an identity, how often rotation is permitted, what the LLM currently believes about attacker motivation, and how threat-responsive rotation should be.

B. Decision Variables — What the Solver Decides
The primary variable x_{i,z,t,p}
This is the only thing RC2 actually decides. Everything else is either derived from it or used to score it. Setting x_{i,z,t,p} = 1 means: "deploy honeypot type kᵢ in zone z during slot t, presenting identity p to the network."
The key insight is that all four dimensions are inseparable in practice. You cannot decide what to deploy without simultaneously deciding where, when, and as whom, because:
	•	Cost depends on zone (not type alone)
	•	Detection credit depends on whether the right type is in the right zone at the right slot
	•	Deception credibility depends on whether the persona is plausible for that zone and asset type
	•	Rotation decisions depend on how long a specific (type, zone, persona) triple has been visible
The derived variables
x_{i,t} — derived as ΣzΣp x_{i,z,t,p} ≥ 1. This collapses the four-dimensional primary variable back to a type-slot pair purely for C4 conflict checking. Two honeypot types that conflict (share monitoring resources, produce redundant signals) cannot both be active in the same slot regardless of zone or persona.
u_{i,z,t} — type-discovery flag. This encodes the attacker's learning about the honeypot as an infrastructure object. Once the same type has been present in zone z for τᵈ consecutive slots under any persona, the attacker has had enough exposure to identify it as a honeypot by its behavioral signature, port fingerprint, or traffic pattern. The "any persona" clause matters: persona rotation does not reset type exposure, because the underlying service behavior (response timing, banner format, protocol implementation) remains the same regardless of what hostname it uses.
u_{i,z,t,p} — persona-discovery flag. This is a separate, finer-grained discovery process. The attacker fingerprints the identity — the hostname, the AD account name, the employee profile. If HR_workstation has been at 10.2.x.y for two months, the attacker's reconnaissance notes say "this is a known endpoint." Rotating to DevOps_server resets this flag independently of u_{i,z,t}, because the identity the attacker mapped no longer exists.
The dual-flag architecture is the key insight. Type discovery and persona discovery are independent processes triggered by different attacker behaviors. A sophisticated attacker does both. The formulation models both and zeroes credit when either fires.

C. Derived Weights — The Value Calculation
The three weight equations transform raw parameters into the values the solver maximizes.
Equation (2): w̃ⱼ,ₐ = wⱼ,ₐ × dₘ,ₐ / hᵈ,ₐ
The 1/hᵈ term is the perimeter-preference mechanism. If an asset is at hop distance 1 from the network entry (i.e., in the DMZ), its detection weight is full. At hop distance 3 (deep in the internal network), its weight is one-third. This encodes the operational truth that catching an attacker at hop 1 prevents all downstream damage, while catching them at hop 3 means they have already traversed two zones. The dₘ,ₐ role multiplier scales further by asset criticality — a database server is worth more than a workstation.
Equation (3): Wⱼ,ₐ = w̃ⱼ,ₐ × (1 + σⱼ)
The (1+σⱼ) stealth factor creates asymmetric value for hard-to-detect techniques. T1048 (Exfiltration over Alternative Protocol) has σ ≈ 0.9 because it is nearly invisible to conventional monitoring. A honeypot covering it earns 1.9× the base weight. This makes the solver seek out coverage of stealthy techniques even when they appear in fewer zones — their rarity makes them more valuable, not less.
Equation (4): PWπ,h,j,a = ρπ × ivπ,h × Wⱼ,ₐ
This combines path probability, hop interception value, and technique-asset weight into a single path-weight score. It is the currency the objective function maximizes. The persona extension multiplies every use of PW by qₚ, which scales the value proportionally to how relevant the current persona is given the LLM's threat assessment.

D. Hard Constraints — What the Solver Cannot Violate
C1 — The foundational detection chain
¬cⱼ,a,t ∨ ⋁ᵢ,ₚ (x_{i,zone(a),t,p} · (1−u_{i,zone(a),t,p}))
This says: if you claim detection of technique tⱼ on asset a at slot t, there must exist at least one deployed honeypot in the correct zone at that slot whose persona has not been discovered. The clause form means the solver cannot claim detection credit (set cⱼ,a,t = 1) without actually committing to a deployment that supports it. The persona-discovery guard (1−u_{i,zone(a),t,p}) is new: even if the type is undiscovered, a blown persona yields zero credit.
C4 and C12 — Two levels of conflict prevention
C4 prevents type conflicts: a web trap and a DNS trap that share monitoring infrastructure cannot both be deployed in the same slot, regardless of zone or persona. This operates at the type level.
C12 prevents persona conflicts: two different honeypot types cannot wear the same persona in the same zone at the same slot. This operates at the identity level. If ssh_trap and db_trap both present as HR_workstation in the DMZ at month 2, an attacker who sees two "HR workstations" immediately knows at least one is fake. The deception collapses not because the honeypot was detected as a honeypot, but because the identity was detected as duplicated. C12 is structurally identical to C4 but operates one dimension deeper.
C5 and C5b — Two levels of plausibility enforcement
C5 enforces physical reachability: a honeypot requiring SSH connectivity cannot be deployed in an air-gapped OT zone regardless of how valuable it would be. This is a hard physical constraint.
C5b enforces mimicry plausibility: the four-condition J-index checks that (servertype, zone, technique, persona) all belong to the generative knowledge set GK_{i,p}. An ssh_trap wearing Finance_DB persona on a Linux endpoint fails this check because the (servertype=Linux, persona=Finance_DB) pair is not in GK — Linux workstations do not run finance database roles in a plausible enterprise topology. Without C5b, the solver could claim deception credit for deployments that no attacker would actually believe.
C8, C9, C13 — The three rotation constraints
These work together to prevent both over-rotation and under-rotation.
C9 (type-discovery) fires after τᵈ consecutive active slots and is not reset by persona changes. It represents the attacker learning the honeypot's behavioral signature.
C13 (persona-rotation) fires after τᵈᵖ consecutive slots with the same persona and is reset by switching to a different identity. It represents the attacker learning the hostname, AD account, or network identity.
C8 (churn limit) bounds the total number of state changes across the planning horizon. Without C8, the solver could rotate every slot — technically avoiding both C9 and C13 — but the operational cost of constant reconfiguration would make the deployment unmanageable. C8 forces the solver to find a rotation schedule that is both evasion-effective and operationally sustainable.
The interaction: C9 says "you must rotate before the type is discovered." C13 says "you must rotate the persona before the identity is discovered." C8 says "but you cannot rotate too frequently." The solver must find the schedule that satisfies all three simultaneously, which is a genuine combinatorial challenge.
C10 and C11 — The temporal persistence constraints
C10 closes the gap-slot vulnerability. A patient attacker who knows a honeypot rotates monthly will simply wait for the unmonitored month and attack then. C10 prevents this by requiring that the critical hop h★ of each path π be covered in at least ⌈ρπ·τ⌉ slots. For a path with probability 0.30 over 4 slots, at least 2 slots must be covered. The attacker cannot rely on a predictable gap.
C11 makes the rotation threshold itself dynamic. When STIX/TAXII signals rising threat probability on path π, τᵈ tightens automatically — τᵈ = τᵈ⁰ · (1 − ρπ/ρₘₐˣ). At ρπ = 0.55 with τᵈ⁰ = 3, τᵈ drops to 1.35, meaning the honeypot must rotate approximately every slot. As the threat subsides, τᵈ relaxes and rotation slows, reducing operational cost. This is real-time threat-responsive behavior encoded directly into the constraint structure rather than requiring a human operator to manually adjust rotation schedules.

E. Soft Clauses — What the Solver Maximizes
The six soft-clause families form a strict priority hierarchy through geometric weights: w₄ = 1000w₃ = 100,000w₂ = 1,000,000w₁. This means the solver will always sacrifice any amount of lower-level credit to gain one unit of higher-level credit.
L4 (Early Interception, w₄): The highest priority. Catching an attacker at a non-final hop earns the maximum interception value for the entire path. The 1000× multiplier ensures the solver always prefers placing one honeypot at hop 1 of a path over accumulating any amount of forensic or technique-coverage credit elsewhere. This encodes the prevention-over-forensics principle as a mathematical guarantee.
L3-fwd (Path Coverage, w₃): Forward path coverage earns full path-weight credit. The ρπ factor means higher-probability paths earn proportionally more, directing the solver toward paths the attacker is actually likely to use.
L3-bwd (Forensic Coverage, 0.7w₃): The same clause structure as L3-fwd but discounted to 70% of its value. This encodes the operational reality that forensic evidence gathered at a final hop — after the target has been reached — is valuable for attribution but cannot undo the compromise. The 0.7 factor is calibrated to keep forensic coverage in the objective without allowing it to crowd out prevention.
L2-tech (Technique Coverage, w₂): Awards a bonus for each distinct ATT&CK technique covered. This creates direct pressure to spread coverage across the technique matrix rather than over-investing in the techniques already covered by the highest-value deployments.
L2-fam (Tactic-Family Breadth, 1.2w₂): The 1.2× multiplier means that covering a new tactic family is worth more than additional coverage within an already-covered family. This prevents the solver from concentrating all coverage in Lateral Movement while leaving Initial Access and Exfiltration unmonitored.
L1 (Basic Detection, w₁): The baseline credit for any detection event. This is the floor that ensures even deployments that cannot contribute to path coverage still earn some value.
The persona extension adds qₚ as a multiplicative factor to every clause and adds dual discovery guards (1−u_type)·(1−u_persona). The qₚ factor means that a deployment wearing a threat-relevant persona (e.g., Finance_DB when the attacker is financially motivated) earns proportionally more credit. The dual guards mean credit immediately drops to zero the moment either discovery flag fires, creating continuous pressure on the solver to maintain both type-rotation and persona-rotation throughout the planning horizon.

F. Cross-Path Force-Multiplier
This is the mechanism that makes the MAXSAT approach structurally superior to any greedy method. When RC2 encounters an UNSAT core — a minimal set of soft clauses that cannot all be simultaneously satisfied — it resolves them together in a single step.
A single ssh_trap deployment in Zone 2 resolves L3 clauses for three paths simultaneously (web→db, OT infiltration, brute→AD) because all three paths share Zone 2 at hop 2. The total credit earned in one solver step is 3.612 versus 1.749 for a greedy method that evaluates only the highest-probability path. The 2.1× advantage per deployment decision compounds across all zones and all types.
The persona extension scales this multiplicatively: if the LLM has set q_p = 0.40 for HR_workstation (up from the default 0.25), every path covered by that deployment earns 0.40/0.25 = 1.6× more credit than at default priors. The cross-path advantage and the persona-prior advantage are independent and compound together.
Greedy methods structurally cannot discover this because they evaluate paths serially. By the time the greedy method reaches path 2, it has already committed budget to path 1 and cannot retroactively recognize that a different deployment would have covered both.

G. Algorithm 1 — The Adaptive Intelligence Layer
Algorithm 1 is the bridge between live threat intelligence and the solver. Its original function was to warm-start RC2 when path probabilities ρπ change — recomputing soft-clause weights without touching hard constraints and re-certifying optimality in under 5 seconds.
The persona extension adds Step 3: updating qₚ from the threat-intel classification. This is where the LLM contribution is most precisely defined. The LLM reads the STIX/TAXII feed and classifies the attacker's motivation — financially motivated, IP-theft focused, destructive, etc. It then adjusts the persona prior vector to reflect which personas are most likely to attract that attacker's attention.
This is a fundamentally different kind of LLM contribution than "nudging weights." Adjusting qₚ produces an operationally interpretable output: "this month, prioritize Finance_DB and HR_workstation personas." A security operator can read that output, understand it, and override it if domain knowledge warrants. Adjusting anonymous w values produces an opaque vector that requires the operator to trust the LLM without being able to verify its reasoning.
